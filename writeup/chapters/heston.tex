\chapter{The Heston Model}

The classic Black-Scholes model makes the strong assumption that the stock returns are normally distributed with known mean and variance. However, the Black-Scholes formula does not depend on the mean of the log returns $\mu$ (since we perform a change of measure, and it depends instead on the risk-free rate $r$). We therefore have two directions in which we can take this project: we can model the risk-free rate as a stochastic process, or we can allow the volatility to vary according to a stochastic process. We will focus on the latter. Heston \cite{heston1993closed} proposes a model involving a stochastic variance process in which a closed-form solution for European call options exists. In this model, the dynamics of the asset price $S$ and the variance (squared volatility) process $V$ are governed by the stochastic differential equation system under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb Q$:
\begin{align}
\mathrm dS_t &= rS_t\mathrm dt + S_t \sqrt {V_t} \mathrm dW_t^1 \\
\mathrm d V_t &= \kappa (\theta - V_t) \mathrm dt + \sigma \sqrt {V_t} \mathrm dW_t^2
\end{align}
where we have initial conditions $S_0 > 0$ and $V_0 \ge 0$, and the two Brownian motions are correlated with
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d\langle W^1, W^2\rangle _t = \rho \,\mathrm dt \qquad \rho \in (-1, 1)
\end{equation}
The dynamics of the variance $V$ follow a Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) process with mean reversion rate $\kappa > 0$, long-run mean $\theta > 0$ and volatility of the volatility $\sigma > 0$. We can enforce the Feller condition $2\kappa\theta \ge \sigma^2$ so that the volatility process stays positive.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../plots/heston/vol_smile.png}
    \caption[Heston Volatility Smile]{Volatility smile generated by the Heston model}
    \label{fig:heston_vol_smile}
\end{figure}

The Heston model takes into account the stochastic nature of volatility while enforcing its mean-reverting nature, which is observed empirically in the market. It supports non-flat volatility smiles, as we can observe in Figure \ref{fig:heston_vol_smile}. The curvature is determined by the volatility of volatility $\sigma$, and the skew is determined by the correlation $\rho$.

\section{Closed-form Solution}

There exists a closed-form solution derived by Heston \cite{heston1993closed} to the price of the European call option (and indeed the put option, by put-call parity). This will serve as a benchmark, which would give us confidence in the accuracy of our other techniques for European options, and by extension, for American options too.

The closed-form solution is derived by using an Ansatz inspired by the Black-Scholes formula, where the unknown functions in the Ansatz can be determined by substituting our Ansatz into the corresponding PDE and boundary conditions. We will include a sketch of the derivation here which follows those found in \cite{heston1993closed, gatheral2011volatility}, but with some further justification for a better overall understanding.

Let $C$ denote the price of the European call option. We will derive this PDE when we derive the variational equation in Section \ref{sec:heston_variation_equation}, where we show that the price of the European call option with strike $K$ and maturity $T$ must satisfy
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial C}{\partial t} + rS \frac{\partial C}{\partial S} + \kappa (\theta - V) \frac{\partial C}{\partial V} + \frac 12 VS^2 \frac{\partial ^2 C}{\partial S^2} + \rho \sigma V S \frac{\partial ^2 C}{\partial S \partial V} + \frac 12 \sigma^2 V \frac{\partial ^2 C}{\partial V^2} - rC = 0
\label{eq:heston_feynman_kac_pde}
\end{equation}
with the terminal condition $C(T, S, V) = (S-K)^+$.

We will mimic the form of the Black-Scholes formula to form the Ansatz for the solution
\begin{equation}
C(S, V, t) = SP_1(x, V, t) - Ke^{-r(T-t)}P_2(x, V, t), \qquad x := \ln S
\label{eq:heston_ansatz}
\end{equation}
with terminal conditions $P_j(x, V, T) = \boldsymbol{1}_{\{x \ge \ln K\}}$ so that our Ansatz reduces to $(S-K)^+$ at maturity. We can substitute the Ansatz into the PDE and equate the coefficients of $S$ and $Ke^{-r(T-t)}$ to obtain two PDEs for $P_1$ and $P_2$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial P_j}{\partial t} + (r + u_jV) \frac{\partial P_j}{\partial x} + (a - b_j V) \frac{\partial P_j}{\partial V} + \frac 12 V \frac{\partial^2P_j}{\partial x^2} + \rho \sigma V \frac{\partial^2 P_j}{\partial x \partial V} + \frac 12 \sigma^2 V \frac{\partial^2 P_j}{\partial V^2} = 0
\end{equation}
where $u_1 = \frac 12$, $u_2 = -\frac 12$, $a = \kappa\theta$, $b_1 = \kappa - \rho \sigma$ and $b_2 = \kappa$ \cite{heston1993closed}. With this in mind, let's define the characteristic functions
\begin{equation}
    \phi_j(x, V, t; \omega) = \mathbb E^{\mathbb Q_j}\left[e^{i\omega x_T}\mid \mathcal F_t\right]
\end{equation}
which admits the Ansatz
\begin{equation}
    \phi_j(x, V, t; \omega) = \exp\left(C_j(\tau, \omega) + D_j(\tau, \omega) V + i \omega x\right), \qquad \tau := T-t
\end{equation}
since the coefficients in the PDE for $P_j$ are independent of $x$ (justifying the $e^{i\omega x}$ term) and affine in $V$ (justifying the $e^{D_j V}$ term) \cite{duffie2000transform}.

We can insert this Ansatz into the PDE for $P_j$ to get a system of ODEs for $C_j$ and $D_j$:
\begin{equation}
    \frac{\partial D_j}{\partial \tau} = \frac 12 \sigma^2 D_j^2 + (\rho \sigma i \omega - b_j)D_j + u_j i \omega - \frac 12 \omega^2, \qquad \frac{\partial C_j}{\partial \tau } = ri\omega + aD_j
\end{equation}
with the boundary conditions $C_j(0, \omega) = D_j(0, \omega) = 0$. There exists a closed-form solution to this system of ODEs, where we first define
\begin{align}
    d_j & := \sqrt{(\rho \sigma i \omega - b_j)^2 - \sigma^2 (2u_j i\omega - \omega^2)} \\
    g_j & := \frac{b_j - \rho \sigma i \omega - d_j}{b_j - \rho \sigma i \omega + d_j}
\end{align}
where we use the opposite sign convention compared to Heston's original formulation for better numerical stability (more precisely to avoid regions of numerical discontinuity in the complex plane) as suggested in \cite{albrecher2007little}. This yields the solutions
\begin{align}
    D_j(\tau, \omega) &= \frac{b_j - \rho\sigma i\omega - d_j}{\sigma^2} \cdot \frac{1 - e^{-d_j\tau}}{1 - g_j e^{-d_j\tau}} \\
    C_j(\tau, \omega) &= ri\omega\tau + \frac a{\sigma^2} \left[(b_j - \rho \sigma i \omega - d_j)\tau - 2\ln \frac{1-g_j e^{-d_j\tau}}{1 -g_j}\right]
\end{align}

By the Gil-Pelaez inversion theorem \cite{gil1951note}, we can retrieve $P_j$ from its characteristic function $\phi_j$:

\begin{equation}
P_j = \frac 12 + \frac 1\pi \int_0^\infty \Re\left[\frac{e^{-i\omega \ln K} \phi_j(x, V, t; \omega)}{i\omega}\right]\,\mathrm d\omega
\label{eq:heston_gil_pelaez}
\end{equation}

which we can substitute back into our original Ansatz for the call option (\ref{eq:heston_ansatz}) to get the closed-form solution for the call option price. If we wanted to be pedantic, we could argue this is not strictly closed-form as we require numerical integration to compute the second term of (\ref{eq:heston_gil_pelaez}), but this remains a semi-analytical and tractable solution nonetheless, that we could use as a benchmark for our other methods.

\section{Tree Solution}

Since the closed-form solution is limited to European options, we would like to find ways to approximate solutions to the pricing problem so that we can eventually extend the case to American options, which have no closed-form solution. The first method we will explore is the tree method, where it is easy to modify to the American case by adding an additional comparison to the intrinsic value at each node. We will use the closed-form formula as a benchmark for the tree method for European options. This would give us confidence in the solution the tree model provides for American options as it is a simple modification in the algorithm, which we can use as a benchmark for the neural networks we will explore later.

The idea will be similar to the binomial tree we explored earlier in the paper for the Black-Scholes model, but we now have two correlated stochastic processes in the Heston model: the asset price process and the variance process. As this tree is not recombinant, generating paths for these processes and computing the option prices by backward induction naively would lead to an exponential time complexity, which is not computationally feasible. Therefore, we will use a grid interpolation approach as in \cite{vellekoop2009tree} so that the number of nodes grows quadratically in the number of time steps instead.

For the algorithm, we will work under the variance process $V$ and log stock price process $Z$ (which is a simple transformation from $S$ using Itô's formula):
\begin{align}
    \mathrm dV_t &= \kappa(\theta - V_t)\mathrm dt + \sigma \sqrt{V_t} \mathrm dW_t^1 \\
    \mathrm d Z_t &= (r-\tfrac 12 V_t)\mathrm dt + \sqrt {V_t} \mathrm dW_t^2
\end{align}
where $\mathrm d\langle W^1, W^2\rangle_t = \rho \,\mathrm dt$ under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb Q$ and $\kappa, \sigma, \theta, r > 0$ as before. Of course, the value $C$ of the European option with maturity $T$ and payoff function in terms of stock and volatility values $g:\mathbb R^+ \times \mathbb R^+ \to \mathbb R$ will be
\begin{equation}
    C(t, V_t, Z_t) = e^{-r(T-t)}\mathbb E^\mathbb Q [g(e^{Z_T}, \sqrt {V_T})\mid \mathcal F_t]
\end{equation}

\subsection{Euler scheme}
To construct the tree, we need to discretise the process. If we have $n$ steps, then $\Delta t = \frac Tn$. Using the standard Euler scheme, we can have
\begin{align}
    V_{k+1} &= V_k + \kappa (\theta - V_k^+)\Delta t + Y^1_{k+1} \sigma \sqrt{V_k^+ \Delta t} \\
    Z_{k+1} &= Z_k + (r - \tfrac 12 V_k^+)\Delta t + Y^2_{k+1} \sqrt{V_k^+ \Delta t}
\end{align}
where the increment variables $(Y_k^1, Y_k^2)$ are iid pairs across time steps $k$ with the joint distribution given by
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb Q^n(Y_k^1 = i_1, Y_k^2 = i_2) = \frac 14 (1 + i_1i_2\rho) \qquad i_1, i_2 \in \{-1, 1\}
\end{equation}
We use a different measure $\mathbb Q^n$ (with $n$ indicating the number of time steps) as it is not the same as the risk-neutral measure in the Heston model. To ensure positivity of the process, we have taken the positive part of the variance process $V_k^+ :=\max(0, V_k)$. Note here, $V_k$ shouldn't be negative anyway since we will enforce the Feller condition in practice, but with this in place we have full generality.

\subsection{Interpolation optimisation}

The problem with the naïve Euler scheme is that the tree is not recombining, and hence the number of nodes scales exponentially with increasing time steps, which is not feasible computationally. We will hence, for each time step $k$, construct a grid of option prices at time $k$ and the backward induction will be a bilinear interpolation of the four nearest points.

To set this up, we need the boundaries of the grid at each time step $k$, which is given by the minimum and maximum values of $V_k$ and $Z_k$ in the support. In other words, we have
\begin{align}
    z_k^\text{max} &= \max \{z : \mathbb Q^n (Z_k = z) > 0\} \\
    z_k^\text{min} &= \min \{z : \mathbb Q^n (Z_k = z) > 0\} \\
    v_k^\text{max} &= \max \{v : \mathbb Q^n (V_k = v) > 0\} \\
    v_k^\text{min} &= \min \{v : \mathbb Q^n (V_k = v) > 0\}
\end{align}
With these boundaries, we have the grid spacing given our mesh sizes $m_z, m_v \in \mathbb N^+$:
\begin{align}
    \Delta z_k &= (z_k^\text{max} - z_k^\text{min}) / m_z \\
    \Delta v_k &= (v_k^\text{max} - v_k^\text{min}) / m_v
\end{align}
and the gridpoints:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\mathcal S}_k = \left\{\left(
    v_k^\text{min} + i\Delta v_k, z_k^\text{min} + j\Delta z_k \right) \Bigm | i \in \{0, \dots, m_v\}, j \in \{0, \dots, m_z\}\right\}
\end{equation}

We will now define the discretised process $(\tilde V, \tilde Z)$ on the grid space $\hat {\mathcal S}_k$. This will involve the Euler scheme functions
\begin{align}
    \nu(y, v, z) &= v + \kappa (\theta - v^+) \Delta t + y \sigma \sqrt{v^+ \Delta t} \\
    \zeta(y, v, z) &= z + (r - \tfrac 12 v^+)\Delta t + y \sqrt{v^+\Delta t}
\end{align}
where we notice $\nu$ doesn't depend on $z$, but we will put $z$ in as an argument nonetheless for a uniform function signature.

Then using these functions, we can retrieve the next step. We can evolve our process to the next step with
\begin{equation}
    (\tilde V_{k+1}, \tilde Z_{k+1}) = \Phi_{J^v_{k+1}, J^z_{k+1}}^{\hat {\mathcal S}_{k+1}}\big(\nu (Y_{k+1}^1, \tilde V_k, \tilde Z_k), \zeta (Y_{k+1}^2, \tilde V_k, \tilde Z_k)\big)
\label{eq:heston_tree_next_step}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_{j_1, j_2}^{\mathcal S} : \mathbb R^2 \to \mathcal S \subset \mathbb R^2$ is a function mapping $(v, z) \in \mathbb R^2$ to one of the grid points in $\mathcal S := \mathcal S_v \times \mathcal S_z$. $j_1, j_2 \in \{0, 1\}$ are additional parameters that encode the snapping direction and determine the corner in which $(v, z)$ is mapped, corresponding to $J^v_{k+1}, J^z_{k+1}$ in (\ref{eq:heston_tree_next_step}). Visually, this is illustrated in Figure \ref{fig:tree_illustration}, where each point in the price-variance space can evolve to four different points in the next time step. For each of these four different points, we will take an interpolation of the four nearest grid points. Hence, we will consider sixteen points in total, some of which may be repeated.

\begin{figure}[htb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.55\textwidth]{../plots/heston/tree_illustration.png}
    \caption[Heston Tree interpolation illustration]{Interpolation illustration (adapted from \cite{vellekoop2009tree})}
    \label{fig:tree_illustration}
\end{figure}

To do this, we define the 1D snapping functions $\phi^{\mathcal T}_i: \mathbb R \to \mathcal T$ where we have the one-dimensional grid points $\mathcal T \subset \mathbb R$ and the snap-right indicator $i \in \{0, 1\}$. The function is defined mathematically as
\begin{align}
    \phi_0^{\mathcal T}(u) &= \max\{u' \in \mathcal T : u' \le u\} \\
    \phi_1^{\mathcal T}(u) &= \min\{u' \in \mathcal T : u' \ge u\}
\end{align}
Our 2D snapping function $\Phi^{\mathcal S}_{j_1, j_2}: \mathbb R^2 \to \mathcal S$ where $ \mathcal S = \mathcal S_v \times \mathcal S_z \subset \mathbb R^2$ is thus
\begin{equation}
    (v, z) \mapsto \big(\phi^{\mathcal S_v}_{j_1}(v), \phi^{\mathcal S_z}_{j_2}(z)\big)
\end{equation}

The joint probabilities \cite{vellekoop2009tree} for $Y_{k+1}$ are given by, for $i_1, i_2 \in \{-1, 1\}, j_1, j_2 \in \{0, 1\}$:
\begin{align}
    q_{i_1, i_2, j_1, j_2}(\tilde V_k, \tilde Z_k)&:=\tilde {\mathbb Q}(Y_{k+1}^1 = i_1, Y_{k+1}^2 = i_2, J_{k+1}^v=j_1, J_{k+1}^z=j_2 \mid (\tilde V_k, \tilde Z_k)) \\
    &= \frac 14 \left(1 + i_1i_2\rho\right)\times c_{j_1,j_2}^{\hat{\mathcal S}_{k+1}} \left(\nu (i_1, \tilde V_k, \tilde Z_k), \zeta (i_2, \tilde V_k, \tilde Z_k)\right)
\end{align}
where we have defined the measure $\tilde{\mathbb Q}$ as an extension of $\mathbb Q^n$ to include the random snapping indicators $J_{k+1}^v, J_{k+1}^z$ as well, with the snapping probabilities given by the interpolation quantity \cite{press2007numerical} on a general grid $\mathcal S = \mathcal S_v \times \mathcal S_z \subset \mathbb R^2$:
\begin{equation}
    c_{j_1,j_2}^{\mathcal S}(v, z) = \tilde v^{j_1}(1-\tilde v)^{1-j_1} \cdot \tilde z^{j_2}(1-\tilde z)^{1-j_2}
\end{equation}
where we have the difference terms
\begin{align}
    \tilde v &= \frac{v - \phi_{0}^{\mathcal S_v}(v)}{\phi_{1}^{\mathcal S_v}(v) - \phi_{0}^{\mathcal S_v}(v)} \\
    \tilde z &= \frac{z - \phi_{0}^{\mathcal S_z}(z)}{\phi_{1}^{\mathcal S_z}(z) - \phi_{0}^{\mathcal S_z}(z)}
\end{align}

By using the fact our grid is evenly spaced at all time steps $k$, this simplifies nicely to
\begin{equation}
    \tilde v = \frac{v - \phi_{0}^{\mathcal S_v}(v)}{\Delta v_k}, \qquad \tilde z = \frac{z - \phi_{0}^{\mathcal S_z}(z)}{\Delta z_k}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Backward induction}

We can now work backwards on the tree, having constructed the grids. Our base case is the final grid, where the price is just the payoff as we are at maturity. For a general time $t_k$ at step $k$ and node $(v, z) \in \hat{\mathcal S}_{k}$, we will generate the four possible steps determined by $i_1, i_2$ each taking values in $\{-1, 1\}$, and evolve our node with
\begin{equation}
    (v, z) \mapsto \big(\nu (i_1, v, z), \zeta (i_2, v, z)\big) =:(v'_{i_1}, z'_{i_2})
\end{equation}
which will be in between four points on the grid $\hat{\mathcal S}_{k+1}$. These can be found by performing the $\Phi$ function on our evolved node: for $j_1, j_2 \in \{0, 1\}$ define the multi-index $\alpha = (i_1, i_2, j_1, j_2)$ and we have
\begin{equation}
    (v_\alpha, z_\alpha) = \Phi^{\hat{\mathcal S}_{k+1}}_{j_1, j_2}(v'_{i_1}, z'_{i_2})
\end{equation}
Each of these will have a known option price. Therefore, we can just compute the discounted expectation of these sixteen nodes (two choices for each element of $\alpha$) for the option price at the original node:
\begin{equation}
    C(t_k, v, z) = e^{-r\Delta t} \sum_{\alpha \in \mathcal A} q_\alpha(v, z)\times C(t_{k+1}, v_\alpha, z_\alpha)
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal A = \{-1, 1\}^2 \times \{0, 1\}^2$.

\subsection{Implementation techniques}

If we implemented this method naively, it would be very difficult for us to use the binomial tree as a benchmark because it would need to construct a new tree from scratch for every single value of $S_0$, $V_0$ and $T$. Thus any heatmaps or plots we create would require hundreds of trees which is not feasible, considering each tree requires many time steps and large mesh sizes to be accurate. 

Thus in addition to vectorising our code for each tree, we will instead consider an initial range $[S_0^\text{min}, S_0^\text{max}]$ and $[V_0^\text{min}, V_0^\text{max}]$ which we will use to construct our grid bounds. More specifically, $z_k^\text{max}$ and $v_k^\text{max}$ will be the up-most path starting with $(S_0^\text{max}, V_0^\text{max})$ instead of simply some individual value $(S_0, V_0)$, and similarly for the down paths. In this way, when we use the binomial tree as a benchmark, we can use any initial value of $(S_0, V_0)$ as long as it's in the original range and compute the price with an interpolation as described above. With this method, we can evaluate across different initial values while constructing just one tree. We sacrifice a little accuracy in doing so because the grids would be more widely spaced, which we can combat by increasing the mesh sizes. Thus, we effectively construct one more complex tree instead of hundreds of simpler ones, which is much more computationally efficient.

As for selecting our mesh sizes, we will use the result that if we have
\begin{equation}
    \lim _{n \to \infty} \Delta t = 0, \qquad \lim _{n \to \infty}\max_{k=1, \dots, n-1} \frac{\Delta v^n_k}{\Delta t_k} = 0, \qquad \lim _{n \to \infty}\max_{k=1, \dots, n-1} \frac{\Delta z^n_k}{\Delta t_k} = 0
\end{equation}
then the process represented in our Heston tree converges weakly to the original process $(V, Z)$ \cite{vellekoop2009tree}. Note here we denote $\Delta v_k$ from before as $\Delta v_k^n$ to emphasise we have split our maturity $T$ into $n$ steps, and similarly for $\Delta z_k^n$.

Bearing this in mind, a resolution of $n=100, m_v=300, m_z=600$ allows our tree to faithfully reproduce the closed-form solution, which is presented in the numerical results in Section \ref{sec:heston_tree_results}.

\section{PINN Solution}

\subsection{Variational equation}
\label{sec:heston_variation_equation}

We would now like to derive the variational equation for options on assets under the Heston model. Note that for a $d$-dimensional general process $X_t \in \mathbb R^d$ solving the Itô SDE
\begin{equation}
\mathrm dX_t = b(X_t) \mathrm dt + \sigma(X_t)\mathrm d W_t
\end{equation}
where we have the drift $b:\mathbb R^d \to \mathbb R^d$, the volatility $\sigma:\mathbb R^d \to \mathbb R^{d \times m}$ and the $m$-dimensional Brownian motion $W_t$. On second-order smooth functions $f \in \mathcal C^2(\mathbb R^d)$, the infinitesimal generator $\mathcal L$ is defined as \cite{bressloff2025sde}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}[f](x) := \lim_{t \to 0}
\frac{\mathbb{E}[f(X_t) \mid X_0=x] - f(x)}{t}
\end{equation}
which exists and can be derived with Itô’s formula (or Taylor’s expansion) to be \cite{pham2009continuous}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}[f](x)
= \sum_{i=1}^d b_i\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}
+ \frac12 \sum_{i=1}^d\sum_{j=1}^d
\Sigma_{ij} \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}
\end{equation}
where $b_i$ is the $i$-th component of the drift vector $\boldsymbol b := b(x)$ and $\Sigma_{ij}$ is the $(i, j)$-th component of the covariance matrix $\Sigma(x) = \boldsymbol \sigma \boldsymbol \sigma^\top$, with $\boldsymbol \sigma := \sigma(x)$.

Let’s apply this to our Heston model. The drift vector is
\begin{equation}
b(S, V) = \begin{pmatrix} rS \\ \kappa (\theta - V)\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
To compute $\sigma\sigma^\top$, we need to represent our correlated Brownian motions in terms of independent ones. Suppose we have independent Brownian motions $B^1$ and $B^2$, we can define
\begin{align}
W^1 &= B^1 \\
W^2 &= \rho B^1 + \sqrt {1-\rho^2}B^2
\end{align}
which has the quadratic variation $\mathrm d\langle W^1, W^2\rangle_t = \rho \mathrm dt$ as required. Using these new Brownian motions, we can rewrite the SDE as
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d \begin{pmatrix}
S_t \\ V_t
\end{pmatrix} = \underbrace{
\begin{pmatrix}
rS_t \\ \kappa(\theta - V_t)
\end{pmatrix}}_{b(S_t, V_t)}\mathrm dt +
\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}
S_t \sqrt{V_t} & 0 \\
\rho\sigma\sqrt{V_t} & \sigma \sqrt{V_t}\sqrt{1 - \rho^2}
\end{pmatrix}
}_{\sigma(S_t, V_t)}
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathrm dB_t^{1} \\
\mathrm dB_t^{2}
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
which means that our volatility matrix is now
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol \sigma = \sigma(S, V) = \begin{pmatrix}
S\sqrt{V} & 0 \\
\rho\sigma\sqrt{V} & \sigma\sqrt{V}\sqrt{1-\rho^2}
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
which means
\begin{equation}
\Sigma = \boldsymbol\sigma\boldsymbol\sigma^\top = \begin{pmatrix}
S^2 V & \rho \sigma SV \\ \rho \sigma SV & \sigma^2 V \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

Note here we have many forms of $\sigma$ which may be confusing: $\sigma$ without any arguments is the volatility of the variance process, $\sigma(S, V)$ is the volatility matrix of the SDE, and $\boldsymbol \sigma$ is the same as $\sigma(S, V)$ but with a bold symbol to distinguish it from the scalar $\sigma$.

We now have everything we need for the formula. For any function $f = f(S, V) \in \mathcal C^2(\mathbb R^2_+)$, we have
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L[f] = rS\frac{\partial f}{\partial S} + \kappa(\theta - V)\frac{\partial f}{\partial V} + \frac 12\left(S^2V \frac{\partial ^2f}{\partial S^2} + 2\rho\sigma SV \frac{\partial ^2f}{\partial S\partial V} + \sigma^2V\frac{\partial ^2f}{\partial V^2} \right)
\end{equation}
This is our infinitesimal generator $\mathcal L$ acting on smooth functions $f$. Suppose we have a European option with payoff $g : \mathbb R^+ \to \mathbb R^+$, then our price function $v(t, S, V)$, the value of the call option with expiry $T$ at time $t$ with $S_t = S$ and $V_t = V$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\mathcal G[v] := rv - \frac{\partial v}{\partial t} - \mathcal L[v] = 0
\end{equation}
with terminal condition $v(T, S, V) = g(S)$. This matches the PDE we had stated in the closed-form solution in (\ref{eq:heston_feynman_kac_pde}).

As a check, we can set $\kappa = 0$ and $\sigma = 0$ so that $\mathrm dV = 0$ and volatility becomes a constant, and our model collapses to what we have been working with before. Our generator becomes
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L[f] = rS\frac{\partial f}{\partial S} + \frac 12 S^2 V_0\frac{\partial ^2f}{\partial S^2}
\end{equation}
which matches our expression (\ref{eq:bs_operator}) from the Black-Scholes section, since $V_0$ is the initial (and hence constant) variance.

We have derived the variational equation for the European option for simplicity, but it can be easily extended for the American case. With the same argument as (\ref{eq:variational_inequality_bs_1d}), the value of the American option must satisfy the PDE
\begin{equation}
    \min\big(\mathcal G[v], v(t, S, V) - g(S) \big) = 0
\label{eq:heston_variational_inequality}
\end{equation}

\subsection{PINN formulation}

We can fit the variational equation (\ref{eq:heston_variational_inequality}) into the PINN framework established in Section \ref{sec:pinns} which will serve as the PDE loss (\ref{eq:pinn_pde}), and all that is left is to set up the loss function and the relevant boundary conditions. It will be very similar to the Black-Scholes case, but as we have an additional dimension $V$ for the variance, we will need additional boundary conditions to ensure regularity in the variance dimension as well. These boundary conditions will be inspired by those given for the European call option in \cite{heston1993closed}, but we will adapt them in our study, as they exhibit different behaviour for American put options.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $t \to T$: the value at expiry is just the intrinsic value, or the payoff of the put option:
    \begin{equation}
        v(T, S, V) = \max (0, K-S)
    \end{equation}
    which will form the terminal loss (\ref{eq:pinn_terminal}) for the PINN.
    \item $S\to 0$: the option value is the strike when the stock is worthless, because we can exercise immediately (for European options we would require a discounting term):
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, 0, V) = K
    \end{equation}
    \item $S\to \infty$: the value of a very far out-of-the-money option is 0, as it is very unlikely for it to expire in-the-money:
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, \infty, V) = 0
    \end{equation}
    \item $V \to 0$: volatility being zero is a slightly more complicated case, as this would only be true instantaneously due to the mean-reverting nature of the variance process, and the fact the variance process is guaranteed to be positive by the Feller condition. The original paper by Heston \cite{heston1993closed} proposes a collapsed first-order PDE, but this turns out to be a bit overkill for the one-dimensional case, as it doesn't yield significantly better results. We will take this case to be that the asset price process becomes approximately deterministic, a simplification of small volatilities, and hence the value of the option can be taken to be the payoff
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, S, 0) = \max(0, K - S)
    \end{equation}
    \item $V \to \infty$: when the volatility is very high, we will take it that it is almost sure that the asset price would be zero at some point before expiry, so the value of the option is the strike:
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, S, \infty) = K
    \end{equation}
    which is the asymptotic condition.
\end{enumerate}

In practice, as we clearly cannot represent infinity, we will settle for large constants for the asset price $S_\text{inf}$ and variance $V_\text{inf}$. The minimum for both of these will be taken to be zero. Thus our domain is
\begin{equation}
    \Omega = (0, T) \times (0, S_\text{max}) \times (0, V_\text{max})
\end{equation}

Note the difference between $S_\text{max}$ and $S_\text{inf}$: the former is the maximum asset price in our sampling domain, and the latter is the value we use for the boundary condition. This also applies to $V_\text{max}$ and $V_\text{inf}$.

The boundary conditions (2)-(5) together will form the boundary loss (\ref{eq:pinn_boundary}) for the PINN. By choosing relevant weights for each of these loss components (we could additionally set different values for different individual boundary components), we can ensure that the neural network learns the correct behaviour at the boundaries, which is crucial for the accuracy of the solution.


\section{Multi-asset PINN}

With the neural network established, we are now ready to move on to pricing options on multiple assets. As with the Black-Scholes case, we will price the put on the product of multiple assets because we can find a one-dimensional reduction. Here, we will consider a simplified model.

Let us have $n$ assets with price processes $S = (S^1, \dots, S^n)^\top$ which are correlated with each other with the $n \times n$ correlation matrix $\Sigma$. Suppose further we have a common variance process $V$ which is correlated with each of the assets. Then this process is governed by the stochastic differential equations:
\begin{align}
    \dd S &= r\diag(S) \,\dd t + \sqrt V \diag(S) \diag(\sigma) L \,\dd W^1 \\
    \dd V &= \kappa(\theta - V)\,\dd t + \bar \sigma \sqrt V \,\dd W^2
\end{align}
where we have the vector of iid Brownian motions $W^1 = (W^{1, 1}, \dots, W^{1, n})^\top$, volatility vector $\sigma = (\sigma_1, \dots, \sigma_n)^\top$ and the lower-triangular Cholesky decomposition of the correlation matrix $L$ such that $\Sigma = LL^\top$ where its diagonal has non-negative entries. This is guaranteed to exist as $\Sigma$ is positive semi-definite (more precisely, if $\Sigma \succ 0$ then $L$ exists and is unique, and if $\Sigma \succeq 0$ then $L$ exists but may not be unique).

For simplicity, in this model we will assume equicorrelation between the assets: that is $\Sigma_{ij} = \varrho$ for $i \ne j$ and $\Sigma_{ii} = 1$. Alternatively, we can write
\begin{equation}
    \Sigma = (1 - \varrho) I_n + \varrho \mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top
\end{equation}
where $I_n$ is the $n$-dimensional identity matrix and $\mathbf{1}$ is the $n$-dimensional vector of ones. We will quickly derive a bound for $\varrho$ to ensure $\Sigma \succeq 0$, so that it is a valid correlation matrix. As $\Sigma$ has an eigenvector $\mathbf{1}$ with corresponding eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 1 + (n-1)\varrho$ and $n-1$ eigenvectors orthogonal to $\mathbf{1}$ with corresponding eigenvalue $\lambda_2 = 1 - \varrho$, we have $\Sigma \succeq 0$ if and only if $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \ge 0$, which gives us the bound $\varrho \in [-\frac 1{n-1}, 1]$.

We will also assume that each independent driving factor is correlated with the variance process: $\dd W^{1, j} \dd W^2 =\rho_j \dd t$, with $\rho = (\rho_1, \dots, \rho_n)^\top$, the cross-correlation vector between assets and the variance process. Note that this means the asset $S^i$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
    \dd S^i = S^i \left(r\,\dd t + \sqrt V \sigma_i \sum_{j=1}^n L_{ij}\,\dd W^{1, j}\right)
\end{equation}

To guarantee positiveness of the variance process, we will enforce the Feller condition on the parameters
\begin{equation}
    2\kappa\theta \ge \bar\sigma^2
\end{equation}

We make these assumptions to allow a one-dimensional reduction for the Heston model, which would provide us with a comparable benchmark for our results with the tree method. This would in turn give us confidence for our approximated solutions, should we wish to relax assumptions or to change the payoff.

\subsection{One-dimensional reduction}

Since we would like to find an equivalent formulation of the product of assets $X:= \prod_{i=1}^n S^i$, it would be easier to work in the log domain. That is, find the governing SDE for the log process $Y = \log X = \sum_{i=1}^n \log S^i$ and then exponentiate with Itô's lemma to recover the desired result.

Let $Y^i = \log S^i$. Then by Itô's lemma, we have
\begin{equation}
    \dd Y^i = \frac 1{S^i} \,\dd S^i - \frac 1{2{S^i}^2} \,\dd \langle S^i\rangle_t
\end{equation}
where ${S^i}^2$ is the square of the price of the $i$-th asset. Here, the quadratic variation term $\dd \langle S^i\rangle_t$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    \dd \langle S^i\rangle_t = {S^i}^2 V \sigma_i^2 \sum_{j=1}^n\sum_{k=1}^n L_{ij}L_{ik} \,\dd \langle W^{1, j}, W^{1, k}\rangle_t = {S^i}^2 V \sigma_i^2 \sum_{j=1}^n L_{ij}^2 \,\dd t
\end{equation}
and we have $\sum_{j=1}^n L_{ij}^2 = \Sigma_{ii} = 1$, thus giving us $\dd \langle S^i\rangle_t = {S^i}^2 V \sigma_i^2 \,\dd t$. Therefore, we have
\begin{equation}
    \dd Y^i = r\,\dd t + \sqrt V\sigma_i \sum_{j=1}^n L_{ij}\,\dd W^{1, j} - \frac 12 V \sigma_i^2 \,\dd t
\end{equation}
We can collect terms and sum over $i$ to get (recalling $Y = \sum_{i=1}^n Y^i$):
\begin{equation}
    \dd Y = \left (nr - \frac 12 V \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2\right) \,\dd t + \sqrt V \sum_{j=1}^n\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i L_{ij} \,\dd W^{1, j}
\end{equation}

For ease of notation, let $A=\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2$ and $b_j = \sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_i L_{ij}$. Then we have
\begin{equation}
    \dd Y = \left(nr - \frac 12 VA\right) \,\dd t + \sqrt V \sum_{j=1}^n b_j \,\dd W^{1, j}
\end{equation}

Now $Y$ has variance
\begin{equation}
    \dd \langle Y\rangle_t = V\sum_{j=1}^n b_j^2 \,\dd t =: VB\,\dd t
\end{equation}
where we let $B := \sum_{j=1}^n b_j^2$ for convenience, we can rewrite $\dd Y$ with a new Brownian motion:
\begin{equation}
    \dd Y = \left(nr - \frac 12 VA\right)\,\dd t + \sqrt {VB} \,\dd W^X, \qquad \dd W^X = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^n b_j \,\dd W^{1, j}}{\sqrt B}
\end{equation}

Note here that $B$ can be expressed directly in terms of $\Sigma$, independently of the choice of the Cholesky factor $L$
\begin{equation}
    B = \sum_{j=1}^n b_j^2 = \|\sigma^\top L\|^2 = \sigma^\top LL^\top \sigma = \sigma^\top \Sigma \sigma
\end{equation}
which means we have
\begin{equation}
    \dd W^X \dd W^2 = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^{n}b_j\rho_j}{\sqrt B}\,\dd t
\end{equation}
so we can derive an explicit formula for the scalar correlation between $W^X$ and $W^2$:
\begin{equation}
    \rho^X = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^n b_j \rho_j}{\sqrt B} = \frac{\sigma^\top L\rho}{\sqrt{\sigma^\top \Sigma \sigma}}
\end{equation}

Using our equicorrelation structure $\Sigma_{ij} = \varrho$ for $i \ne j$ and $\Sigma_{ii} = 1$, we have the nice simplification for $B$:
\begin{equation}
    B = \sigma^\top\Sigma\sigma = (1-\varrho)A + \varrho \left(\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i\right)^2
\end{equation}

Now, we have everything we need to recover $X = e^Y$. Using Itô's lemma again, we have
\begin{align}
    \dd X &= X\,\dd Y + \frac 12 X \,\dd \langle Y\rangle_t \\
    &= X\left( nr + \frac 12 V(B-A)\right)\,\dd t + X\sqrt{VB}\,\dd W^X
\end{align}

This is close to the Heston model, with the diffusion $\tilde V = VB$ which gives us
\begin{align}
    \dd\tilde V &= B\,\dd V \\
    &= \kappa(B\theta - \tilde V)\,\dd t + \bar\sigma \sqrt B \sqrt{\tilde V} \,\dd W^2
\end{align}
which is a new CIR process $\tilde V$ with parameters $\tilde \kappa = \kappa$, $\tilde \theta = B\theta$ and $\tilde \sigma = \bar \sigma \sqrt B$ (as $B$ is constant). The product of assets nearly gives a direct reduction to a one-dimensional Heston process, but the drift of $X$ contains $V$ which is a stochastic process itself. Thus for our benchmark, we will set $\varrho = 0$ which means $B = A$ and the drift term becomes deterministic. The last thing to check is the Feller condition for $\tilde V$, which is satisfied as
\begin{equation}
    2\tilde\kappa \tilde \theta \ge \tilde \sigma^2 \iff 2 \kappa B \theta \ge \bar \sigma^2 B \iff 2 \kappa \theta \ge \bar \sigma^2
\end{equation}
As such, we are able to recover a one-dimensional Heston model for the product of assets $X$ with variance $\tilde V$ and drift $nr$.

\subsection{Variational equation}

Recall for a process $X_t \in \mathbb R^n$ solving $\mathrm dX_t = b(X_t)\mathrm dt + \sigma(X_t)\mathrm dW_t$, the infinitesimal generator $\mathcal L$ can be derived with Itô's formula \cite{pham2009continuous} on $f(x)$ to be
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}[f](x)
= \sum_{i=1}^n \big(b(x)\big)_i \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}
+ \frac12 \sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n
\left(\sigma(x)\sigma(x)^\top\right)_{ij} \frac{\partial ^2 f}{\partial x_i\partial x_j}
\label{eq:generator_general}
\end{equation}

In our case, we have a function $f = f(S^1, \dots, S^n, V)$, and we can derive the infinitesimal generator much like the 1D case, but this time we need to sum over our different asset and variance processes. The drift vector is easy to see by looking at the SDEs, which is
\begin{equation}
    b(S, V) = \begin{pmatrix}
        rS^1 \\ \vdots \\ rS^n \\ \kappa(\theta - V)
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

We need to be more careful with the variance matrix, as our Brownian motions are correlated, and we need to write a variance matrix in terms of iid Brownian motions. Since $W^{1, 1}, \dots, W^{1, n}$ are already independent of each other, we only need to handle the correlation $\dd \langle W^{1, j}, W^2\rangle_t = \rho_j \dd t$. Consider
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm dW^2 = \sum_{j=1}^n \rho_j \mathrm dW^{1, j} + \sqrt{1 - |\rho|^2}\,\mathrm dB^{n+1}
\end{equation}

where we have $|\rho|^2 = \sum_{j=1}^n \rho_j^2$ and where $B^{n+1}$ is a 1-dimensional Brownian motion, independent of all $W^{1, j}$. Clearly we need $|\rho|^2 \le 1$ for this to be well-defined. Note this is quite a strong condition as it means the variance process cannot be too strongly correlated with all the assets at the same time, so care must be taken to ensure this holds before proceeding in practice.

We can easily verify that this satisfies the quadratic variation
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d \langle W^{1, j}, W^2\rangle_t = \rho_j \mathrm dt, \qquad \mathrm d \langle W^2, W^2\rangle_t = \mathrm dt
\end{equation}
as needed. This gives us the needed $n+1$ independent driving Brownian motions: we have $(B^1, \dots, B^{n+1})$, with $B^{i} = W^{1, i}$ for $i = 1, \dots, n$.

We now rewrite our SDEs in the multi-asset Heston model in terms of these independent Brownian motions. The assets $S^i$ are not driven by $B^{n+1}$ so we simply have
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm d S^i = rS^i \mathrm dt + S^i \sqrt V \sigma_i \sum_{j=1}^n L_{ij} \mathrm d B^j + 0 \cdot \mathrm d B^{n+1}
\end{equation}
and then we can substitute the decomposition of $\mathrm dW^2$ into the variance process to get
\begin{align}
    \mathrm dV = \kappa(\theta - V) \mathrm dt + \bar \sigma \sqrt V \sum_{j=1}^n \rho_j \mathrm d B^j + \bar\sigma \sqrt V \sqrt{1 - |\rho|^2}\,\mathrm d B^{n+1}
\end{align}
We can now read off the $(n+1) \times (n+1)$ variance matrix
\begin{equation}
    \sigma(S, V) = \begin{pmatrix}
        \sqrt V \,\text{diag}(S) \,\text{diag}(\sigma) L & 0_n \\
        \bar \sigma \sqrt V \rho^\top & \bar\sigma \sqrt V \sqrt{1 - |\rho|^2}
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
which is written in block form: $\sqrt V \text{diag}(S)\text{diag}(\sigma)L$ is the $n\times n$ block for the assets, $0_n$ is the $n$-dimensional zero vector for the lack of contribution of the variance Brownian motion in the assets (due to independence), $\bar\sigma \sqrt V \rho^\top$ is the $1\times n$ block of the contribution of the asset Brownian motions to the variance process, and $\bar\sigma \sqrt V \sqrt{1 - |\rho|^2}$ is the $1\times 1$ block for the contribution of the variance Brownian motion to the variance process. We can then compute the covariance matrix $\tilde \Sigma$ (to differentiate it from $\Sigma$ for the asset correlations) as
\begin{equation}
    \tilde \Sigma = \sigma(S, V)\sigma(S, V)^\top = \begin{pmatrix}
        V \text{diag}(S) \text{diag}(\sigma) \Sigma \text{diag}(\sigma) \text{diag}(S) & V\bar\sigma \text{diag}(S) \text{diag}(\sigma) L \rho \\
        V\bar\sigma (L\rho)^\top \text{diag}(\sigma) \text{diag}(S) & \bar\sigma^2 V
    \end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}

Therefore, by plugging it into the formula (\ref{eq:generator_general}), our generator can be expressed to be
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \mathcal L[f] &=
            \sum_{i=1}^n rS^i \frac{\partial f}{\partial S^i}
            + \kappa(\theta - V)\frac{\partial f}{\partial V}
            + \frac 12 V \sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n \sigma_i \sigma_j \Sigma_{ij} S^i S^j \frac{\partial^2f}{\partial S^i\partial S^j} \\
            &+ \frac 12 \bar\sigma^2 V \frac{\partial^2f}{\partial V^2}
            + \sum_{i=1}^n \bar\sigma \sigma_i (L \rho)_i V S^i \frac{\partial^2f}{\partial S^i\partial V}
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

For any payoff $g(\cdot)$, we have, as before, the variational equation for a European option being
\begin{equation}
\mathcal G[f] := rf - \frac{\partial f}{\partial t} - \mathcal L[f] = 0
\end{equation}
and for an American option, we can introduce the early stopping criterion and the variational equation becomes
\begin{equation}
\min (\mathcal G[f], f - g) = 0
\end{equation}

\subsection{PINN formulation}

This will be very similar to the one-dimensional case, but we will make a few modifications to account for the additional dimensions. The PDE loss will be the same as before, but the boundary conditions will include:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item $t \to T$: the value at expiry is just the intrinsic value which is the payoff:
    \begin{equation}
        v(T, S^1, \dots, S^n, V) = \max \left(0, K - \prod_{k=1}^n S^k\right)
    \end{equation}
    which serves as our terminal loss (\ref{eq:pinn_terminal}) for the PINN.
    \item $S \to 0$: if one of the assets reaches zero, then the product of assets is consequently zero and we should exercise immediately. Thus the value is the strike. To implement this, let $\tilde S$ be $S$ with one of the assets modified manually to $0$. Then we have
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, \tilde S, V) = K
    \end{equation}
    \item $S \to \infty$: if one of the assets has a very high price, then the product of assets would also be very high meaning the option would expire worthless out-of-the-money. To implement this, let $\bar S$ be $S$ with one of the assets modified manually to $S_\text{inf}$, a large constant. Then the boundary condition is
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, \bar S, V) = 0
    \end{equation}
    \item $V \to 0$: the simplification in the one-dimensional case doesn't work well in practice for the multi-asset case, so we will consider the degenerate PDE as proposed in the original paper by Heston \cite{heston1993closed}, where we have the complementary condition
    \begin{equation}
        \min \left(rv -\frac{\partial v}{\partial t} - r \sum_{i=1}^n S^i \frac{\partial v}{\partial S^i} - \kappa \theta \frac{\partial v}{\partial V}, v - g \right) = 0
    \end{equation}
    where we evaluate $v$ at $(t, S, 0)$.
    \item $V \to \infty$: with infinite volatility, we could enforce the asymptotic condition as in the one-dimensional case, where
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, S, V_\text{inf}) = K
    \end{equation}
    for some large constant $V_\text{inf}$. However, in practice, the PINN struggles to learn this boundary condition, and hence we will use a softer version of this constraint, taking into account the first order:
    \begin{equation}
        \frac{\partial v}{\partial V}(t, S, V_\text{max}) = 0
    \end{equation}
    using the fact that the value of the option flattens as volatility increases, since our practical implementation of $V_\text{max}$ is not large enough to be in the asymptotic region, and using $V_\text{inf}$ creates a large gap between the practical domain and the asymptotic region, decreasing the accuracy of the solution in the practical domain.
\end{enumerate}

We can see that the weakness of the PINN is along the strike price with $S^1=S^2=\sqrt{K}$, and the accuracy is better for lower volatilities. Overall, the results are okay, given that we are sacrificing some accuracy in order to be able to price along the whole input domain, but we will expect the PINN to struggle more with estimating free boundaries correctly since there is a bias in the prices already.

Finally, we would like to check how the PINN captures the price relationship between different initial variances and time to maturity, which is shown in the right figure of Figure \ref{fig:heston_nd_comparison_heatmap} where we plot the option price across $t$ and $V$ with $S^1=S^2=\sqrt{K}$. We can see that this relationship is captured less well: the PINN prefers to give roughly the same price across this cross-section, which is a side-effect of our loss function construction. Since the PDE loss is averaged across the domain, the network settles for giving the average price across this cross-section as it is easier to satisfy the PDE in this way, and the boundary conditions are not strong enough to force the network to learn the correct relationship across this cross-section. To remedy this, we should give more attention to how the weights of the loss components are set, which requires a lot of tuning and experimentation, which is less feasible in this case with each model taking several hours to train, but is something that could be further explored with more computational resources.

Even though the accuracy of the option price for the multi-asset case is not perfect, there is still some value in estimating the free boundary for the multi-asset case, which we will do in the next section.

\subsection{Free boundary}

\subsubsection{One-dimensional case}

\begin{figure}[b]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../plots/heston/heston_1d_continuation_probs.png}
    \caption[Heston PINN continuation probabilities (1D)]{Continuation probabilities for the American put option on the $S$-$V$ plane}
    \label{fig:heston_1d_continuation_probs}
\end{figure}

We will first present the free boundary for the one-dimensional case. As this is a boundary on three dimensions, we will present it as a cross section, where one variable will be fixed at its default value ($t=0, S=1.0, V=0.04$) and the other two will be plotted against each other. This would be cleaner and more interpretable than a colour-coded 3D plot. We will omit the $t$-$S$ plane as it will look very similar to the free boundary for the Black-Scholes case \ref{fig:bs_continuation_probs}, and we will also omit the $t$-$V$ plane as it is not very informative, with continuation probabilities close to $1$ for the whole region near the money at initial time regardless of the initial variance. This leaves us the free boundary on the $S$-$V$ plane, shown in Figure \ref{fig:heston_1d_continuation_probs}.

We can see that the continuation region is reasonably well approximated with both methods, and we additionally observe some noise near the boundary which is due to the Monte Carlo estimation of the continuation value. Unfortunately, the network struggles to capture the curvature characteristic of the free boundary, which is unsurprising as it doesn't price the option with perfect accuracy in all areas. That said, the results are promising against the benchmark, even though we have a patch of the continuation probabilities that are closer to one further in-the-money (at around $(S, V) = (0.4, 0.3)$), which is due to the bias in the option price in the corresponding region when we had our price comparison heatmaps in Figure \ref{fig:heston_1d_comparison_heatmap}.

\subsubsection{Multi-asset case}

We will first present the free boundary on the $S^1$-$S^2$ plane, where we fix $V = V_0$ and $t=0$, as shown in Figure \ref{fig:heston_nd_continuation_probs}. This should be somewhat analogous to the Black-Scholes multi-asset free boundary as observed in Figure \ref{fig:bs_nd_continuation_probs}.

We can see that the continuation region is well approximated, with the shape correctly captured and the continuation probabilities resembling that of the tree method. This is promising: the PINN is able to capture the free boundary well with respect to the asset price.

We now present the free boundary on the $S$-$t$ plane in Figure \ref{fig:heston_nd_continuation_probs_time}, where we fix $S^1 = S^2$ and $V=V_0$. We first notice that the Heston tree free boundary is no longer smooth: this is likely due to the fact we are using an interpolation method which has some degree of error. The PINN also noticeably struggles a bit more to capture the curvature of the free boundary.

These shortcomings are likely linked to the limitations of the model, which has settled for an average price prediction across the time cross section, as an efficient and easy way of minimising the loss. To remedy this, we would need to do further experimentation with different training conditions. Even after changing the activation function between Sigmoid, Tanh and ReLU, trying different weights for the loss components, and even adding dropout layers, the results seem to be somewhat similar, so further improvements to the free boundary estimation would require a more fundamental change to the model, whether it be increasing its capacity or the sampling algorithm.

\chapter{Black-Scholes Model}

The Black-Scholes model \cite{black1973pricing} is the fundamental model for option pricing, which assumes that the underlying asset price follows a geometric Brownian motion. Here, the market consists of two underlying assets:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The risk-free asset, described by a deterministic function
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm d B_t = rB_t \mathrm dt
    \label{eq:bs_risk_free_asset}
    \end{equation}
    where for convenience, we set $B_0 = 1$ and $r$ is the constant risk-free interest rate with continuous compounding. This has the solution $B_t = e^{rt}$ for $t \ge 0$.
    \item The risky asset $S_t$, which has the price process of a geometric Brownian motion (GBM), which satisfies the stochastic differential equation
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm dS_t = \mu S_t \mathrm dt + \sigma S_t \mathrm dW_t \qquad 0 \le t \le T
    \end{equation}
    for a given drift $\mu$ and volatility $\sigma$. We can solve this SDE to yield the unique solution
    \begin{equation}
        S_t = S_0 \exp \left(\left(\mu - \frac 12 \sigma^2\right) t + \sigma W_t\right)\qquad 0 \le t \le T
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

Under risk-neutral pricing, we can find an equivalent probability measure $\mathbb Q$ under which the discounted stock price is a martingale, and hence all assets earn the risk-free rate in expectation after discounting. Under the risk-neutral measure, the SDE for the risky asset becomes
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm dS_t = r S_t \mathrm dt + \sigma S_t \mathrm dW_t^\mathbb Q
\end{equation}
where the drift has been replaced by the risk-free rate $r$. The derivation can be found in Appendix \ref{sec:change_of_measure}.

To price the put option with the tree method, we will use the binomial discrete-time model. This is an abstraction of the market dynamics, where the stock price can only move to one of two possible new prices in a time step. This is a simple model which we can make recombinant so that the algorithm performs with quadratic time complexity, which will serve as a benchmark for our numerical results for other methods.

To formalise the tree construction, let’s fix a time horizon $T > 0$. For some depth $n \ge 1$, divide the interval $[0, T]$ into $n$ even steps each of length $\Delta t = T/n$. Our asset $S_t$ will follow a multiplicative binomial evolution:
\begin{equation}
    S_{i+1} = \begin{cases}
    S_i u & \text{with probability }p \\S_i d & \text{with probability }1-p
    \end{cases}\qquad S_0 > 0
\end{equation}
where $u$ and $d$ are the model parameters determining the two factors of the stock price change per time step, where we choose $u > 1$ and $d \in (0,1)$.

As with (\ref{eq:bs_risk_free_asset}), the financial market will also consist of a risk-free asset $B_t$, discretised so that it satisfies
\begin{equation}
    B_{i+1} = B_i e^{r\Delta t} \qquad B_0 = 1
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the continuously-compounded risk-free rate, typically considered interest. For an arbitrage free market, we should additionally ensure that
\begin{equation}
u > e^{r\Delta t} > d
\end{equation}
for sufficiently small $\Delta t$, else we can make a riskless profit by shorting the stock and investing in the risk-free asset, or vice versa.

To ensure the absence of arbitrage, we require the discounted asset price to have zero drift (admitting a martingale measure). More precisely, let the discounted price be
\begin{equation}
\tilde S_i = e^{-ri\Delta t} S_i
\end{equation}
We require some probability measure $\mathbb Q$, equivalent to the physical measure $\mathbb P$ (have the same non-zero support) such that
\begin{equation}
\mathbb E_\mathbb Q [\tilde S_{i+1} | \mathcal F_i] = \tilde S_i
\end{equation}
To calculate the risk-neutral probability, we can apply this to our model:
\begin{equation}
e^{-r(i+1)\Delta t}\big[pS_i u + (1-p)S_i d \big] = e^{-ri\Delta t}S_i
\end{equation}

Collect the $p$ terms and cancel out the discount factor to get
\begin{equation}
p(S_i u - S_i d) + S_i d = e^{r\Delta t} S_i
\end{equation}

Divide both sides by $S_i$ and rearrange to make $p$ the subject to obtain
\begin{equation}
p = \frac{e^{r \Delta t} -d}{u-d}
\end{equation}
which we will use in our binomial model.

We will adopt a backward induction approach to price the American put at time $0$, since we already know the price at maturity, which is simply the payoff $g: \mathbb R^+ \to \mathbb R$ of the put option:

\begin{equation}
g(S_T) = (K - S_T)^+
\end{equation}

As the discounted price process is a martingale under the risk-free measure $\mathbb Q$, the arbitrage-free value $v(i\Delta t)$ of the derivative at time $i \Delta t$ is necessarily

\begin{equation}
v_i = \underbrace{e^{-r(T-i\Delta t)}}_\text{discount} \,\mathbb E_\mathbb Q[g(S_T)\,|\,\mathcal F_i]
\end{equation}

We will utilise the Cox-Ross-Rubinstein (CRR) Model \cite{cox1979option} for our implementation, which chooses the parameters
\begin{equation}
u = e^{\sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}}\qquad d = e^{-\sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}}
\end{equation}
satisfying the arbitrage free condition, and normalising the local variance of returns to the volatility parameter $\sigma^2$. They also conveniently multiply to $1$.

To implement this, we need to first compute the price tree. This would be, for the price at step $i$ after $j$ up-moves (so clearly $i \ge j$)
\begin{equation}
S_{i, j} = S_0 u^j d^{i-j}
\end{equation}

As the payoff is known at maturity, we can simply set
\begin{equation}
v_n(j) = g(S_{n, j}) \qquad 0 \le j \le n
\end{equation}
and recursively set
\begin{equation}
v_i(j) = e^{-r\Delta t} \big(p^* v_{i+1}(j+1) + (1-p^*)v_{i+1}(j)\big)
\end{equation}
for $0 \le j \le i$. This would be it for European options, but since early exercise is allowed, we will compare this value with the payoff of the price at step $i$ with $j$ up-moves, which is just $f(S_{i, j})$. Thus the value is given by
\begin{equation}
v_i(j) = \max\bigg(e^{-r\Delta t} \big(p^* v_{i+1}(j+1) + (1-p^*)v_{i+1}(j)\big), \; g(S_{i, j})\bigg)
\end{equation}
The root value $v_0(0)$ would be the arbitrage-free option price.

\section{Physics-Informed Neural Networks}

In order to use the framework of PINNs as detailed in section \ref{sec:pinns}, we need to first derive the PDE satisfied by the option price, as well as the boundary conditions. We will first work with the one-dimensional case, then discuss the extension to the multi-dimensional case and how we could build a benchmark to against which we evaluate our results.

\subsection{PINN formulation for one-dimensional case}

In order to set up our PINN, we need to first derive the PDE satisfied by the option price. Recall the value function $v: [0, T] \times \mathbb R^+ \to \mathbb R$ of an American put with payoff $g: \mathbb R^+ \to \mathbb R$ at time $t$ with asset price $S_t = S$ is given by

\begin{equation}
    v(t, S) = \sup_{\tau \in [t, T]} \mathbb E_t\big[e^{-r(\tau - t)}g(S_\tau)\big]
\label{eq:american_option_value_function}
\end{equation}

where $\tau$ is any stopping time in $[t, T]$ and $\mathbb E_t$ is conditional expectation at time $t$, given $S_t = S$. For an American option, we always have two options:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Immediately exercise the option and get payoff $g(S)$. Since this is not necessarily optimal, the payoff must bound the value option from below:
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, S) \ge g(S)
    \end{equation}
    \item Do nothing and let the option continue to time $t + h$ with $h>0$ a very small number. By using the supremum definition of $v(t, S)$ (\ref{eq:american_option_value_function}) and swapping the expectation and supremum \cite{zheng2025fundamentals} we arrive at
    \begin{equation}
        e^{rh}v(t, S) \ge \mathbb E_t \big[v(t+h, S_{t + h})\big]
    \label{eq:continuation_value_inequality}
    \end{equation}
    and the inequality turns into an equality at the limit $\delta \to 0$.
    
\end{itemize}

We will additionally assume that the value function $v \in C^{1,2}$ (continuously differentiable with respect to $t$ and twice continuously differentiable with respect to $S$) so that we can apply Itô's formula. Let's model the asset price $S$ as a geometric Brownian motion satisfying

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm dS_t = rS_t \mathrm dt + \sigma S_t \mathrm dW_t
\end{equation}

Then by Ito’s lemma and taking expectations of both sides we have

\begin{equation}
    \mathbb E\big[v(t +\delta, S_{t+h})\big] = v(t, S) + \mathbb E_t\bigg[\int_t^{t+h} \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(u, S_u) + \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(u, S_u) r S_u + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(u, S_u) \sigma^2 S_u^2\right)\mathrm du\bigg]
\end{equation}

Now let’s substitute (\ref{eq:continuation_value_inequality}) into the LHS, which gives us

\begin{equation}
    e^{rh}v(t, S) \ge v(t, S) + \mathbb E_t\bigg[\int_t^{t+h} \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(u, S_u) + \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(u, S_u) r S_u + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(u, S_u) \sigma^2 S_u^2\right)\mathrm du\bigg]
\end{equation}

We can use the Taylor approximation $e^{rh} = 1 + rh + o(h)$, which permits us to cancel the $v(t, S)$ on both sides. Dividing both sides by $h$ in the same step, we get

\begin{equation}
    rv(t, S) + \frac{o(h)}{h} \ge \mathbb E_t\bigg[\frac 1h\int_t^{t+h} \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(u, S_u) + \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(u, S_u) r S_u + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(u, S_u) \sigma^2 S_u^2\right)\mathrm du\bigg]
\end{equation}

Sending $h \to 0$ makes the higher order term $o(h)/h$ vanish, and the expectation of the integral to collapse to the integrand. Thus we obtain

\begin{equation}
    rv(t, S) \ge \frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(t, S) + \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(t, S) r S + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(t, S) \sigma^2 S^2
\end{equation}

or for simplicity, we define the linear parabolic operator $\mathcal L$ \cite{zhao2025fractional}:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L[v] = rv(t, S) - \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(t, S) r S - \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(t, S) \sigma^2 S^2
\end{equation}

Thus we can concisely express this inequality as

\begin{equation}
    \min\left(-\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(t, S) + \mathcal L[v], v(t, S) - g(S)\right) \ge 0
\end{equation}

However, note that at time $t$, we must either exercise the option or do nothing and continue. One of these two options must be optimal, implying that one of the arguments must be $0$. Thus we actually have an equality:

\begin{equation}
    \min\left(-\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(t, S) + \mathcal L[v], v(t, S) - g(S)\right) = 0
\label{eq:variational_inequality_bs_1d}
\end{equation}

which is the variational equation \cite{jo2019variational} for the American option. Note this is exactly the formulation of the PINN problem (\ref{eq:pinn_pde}).

We are now ready to derive the boundary conditions. Our option has a terminal payoff at time $T$ given by $g(S)$, so we have the terminal condition

\begin{equation}
    v(T, S) = g(S)
\end{equation}

which satisfies the form of the terminal condition for PINNs (\ref{eq:pinn_terminal}). For the spatial boundary conditions, we have two boundaries to consider: $S \to 0$ and $S \to \infty$. In practice, we will have to truncate the domain to $[S_\text{min}, S_\text{max}]$ for some sufficiently small $S_\text{min}$ and sufficiently large $S_\text{max}$. At the lower boundary, we have the boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v(t, S_\text{min}) = g(S_\text{min})
\end{equation}
because it is always optimal to exercise the option when the asset is at its lowest price. At the upper boundary, we assume the option is too far out of the money to ever finish in the money, and hence the option price is approximately $0$. Thus we have the boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v(t, S_\text{max}) = 0
\end{equation}
These three conditions together would form the boundary setup (\ref{eq:pinn_boundary}) for our PINN.

\subsection{Reduction of multi-asset to one-asset case}

Before we apply the PINN framework to the multi-dimensional case, we will first introduce a benchmark for the multi-dimensional case, from which we will build the boundary conditions for the PINN, as they change depending on the payoff function. The benchmark will need to be able to be reduced to a one-dimensional problem, so that we can solve it using the binomial tree method as a reference for our PINN results. This will, in turn, give us confidence that our PINN implementation is correct, and that the results we get for more complex payoffs are reasonable.

In the multi-dimensional case, we have $k$ underlying assets with price processes following a correlated geometric Brownian motion. Mathematically, let each asset $S^i$ satisfy under the risk-free measure $\mathbb Q$, for $i = 1, \dots, k$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm dS_t^i = rS_t^i\mathrm dt + \sigma_i S^i_t \mathrm dW_t^i
\end{equation}
with $\langle W^i, W^j\rangle_t := \mathbb E\big[\mathrm dW_t^i \mathrm dW_t^j\big] = \rho_{ij}\mathrm dt$ for all $i, j$ pairs. As the geometric Brownian motion is multiplicative, for our benchmark, we will consider the put on the product of the assets. More precisely, we consider the put option with payoff
\begin{equation}
    g(S^1, \dots, S^k) = \left(K - \prod_{i=1}^k S^i\right)^+
\end{equation}

To represent the dynamics of the product of the assets, we can apply Ito’s formula to the log of the price process. The log representation is
\begin{equation}
\ln S_t^i = \ln S_0^t + \int_0^t \left (r - \frac 12 \sigma^2_i\right) \,\mathrm ds + \int_0^t \sigma _i \,\mathrm dW_s^i
\end{equation}

Define our new asset $X$ to be
\begin{equation}X_t := \prod_{i=1}^k S^i_t\end{equation}
and hence we have
\begin{equation}
\ln X_t = \sum_{i=1}^k \ln S_t^i = \ln X_0 + \int_0^t \left(kr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2 \right)\,\mathrm ds + \sum_{i=1}^k \int_0^t \sigma_i \,\mathrm dW_s^i
\end{equation}

By taking the differential we have
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d\ln X_t = \left(kr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2 \right)\,\mathrm dt + \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i \,\mathrm dW_s^i
\end{equation}

The quadratic variation of the martingale term is
\begin{equation}
\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i W^i, \sum_{j=1}^k \sigma_j W^j\right\rangle_t = \sum_{i=1}^k\sum_{j=1}^k \sigma_i\sigma_j \rho_{ij} t =: \sigma^2_X t
\end{equation}
i.e. $\sigma_X^2 = \sum_{i=1}^k\sum_{j=1}^k \sigma_i\sigma_j\rho_{ij}$. So we can express the second term in terms of a new Brownian motion $\widetilde W$:

\begin{equation}
\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i \,\mathrm dW_s^i =  \mathrm d\widetilde W_t
\end{equation}

Going back, we have
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d \ln X_t = \left( kr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2\right)\,\mathrm dt + \sigma_X \mathrm d \widetilde W_t
\end{equation}

Exponentiating using Ito’s formula gets us
\begin{equation}
\mathrm dX_t = X_t\left[\left(kr -\frac12\sum_{i=1}^k\sigma_i^2\right)\mathrm dt + \sigma_X\mathrm d\widetilde W_t\right]
+ \frac12 X_t\sigma_X^2 \mathrm dt
\end{equation}
which we can rearrange to get
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d X_t = \left(kr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2 + \frac 12 \sigma_X^2\right) X_t \mathrm dt + \sigma _X X_t \,\mathrm d\widetilde W_t
\end{equation}
which is a geometric Brownian motion with volatility $\sigma_X$ and drift $r_X$ where
\begin{align*}
    r_X &= kr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2 + \frac 12 \sigma_X^2 \\
    &= kr + \sum_{i < j} \sigma_i\sigma_j\rho_{ij}
\end{align*}
by matching coefficients. We can verify the equivalence by simulating many price paths for the assets and comparing their product against the simulations of price paths for the equivalent asset. Using the initial values
\begin{equation}
r = 0.05 \qquad S_0 = (10, 5, 3)^\top \qquad \sigma = (0.2, 0.3, 0.1)^\top \qquad \mathrm R = \begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 0.6 & -0.2 \\ 0.6 & 1 & 0.4 \\-0.2 & 0.4 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm R$ is the correlation matrix, we can plot the histogram of final prices in Figure [\ref{fig:gbm_histogram}].

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{../plots/introduction/gbm_histogram.png}
    \caption{Histogram of final prices for product of assets and equivalent asset}
    \label{fig:gbm_histogram}
\end{figure}

They look very similar, and we can further statistically test if they come from the same distribution. Using Kolmogorov-Smirnov's 2-sample test on the null hypothesis that they come from the same distribution, we obtain a p-value of $0.8491$, which does not provide sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis, which gives us stronger evidence that our derivation is correct.

Therefore, we will be able to price the put option on the product of multiple assets and compare that to the price of the put option on this single constructed asset as a benchmark.

\subsection{PINN formulation for multi-dimensional case}

With the benchmark in place, we now need to derive the variational inequality for the multi-dimensional case where we have $k$ assets. This is a straightforward extension of the one-dimensional case, where the value function $v(t, S^1, \dots, S^k)$ now takes $k+1$ inputs. For notation, we will express the multiple assets as a vector $\mathbf S = (S^1, \dots, S^k)^\top$, so the value function is $v(t, \mathbf S)$. Similarly, the payoff function will be written as $g(S^1, \dots, S^k) = g(\mathbf{S})$.

The value function stays

\begin{equation}
    v(t, \mathbf S) = \sup_{\tau \in [t, T]} \mathbb E_t\big[e^{-r(\tau - t)}g(\mathbf S_\tau)\big]
\end{equation}

Should we choose not to exercise the option at time $t$, we can apply the same argument as in the one-dimensional case: considering a small $h > 0$, we can apply Itô's lemma on multiple dimensions to yield

\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        v(t + h, \mathbf S_{t + h}) = v(t, \mathbf S) + \int_t^{t+h} &\bigg(
            \frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(u, \mathbf S_u) +
            % \mathcal L_u[v(\mathbf S_u)]
            \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{\partial v}{\partial S^i}(u, \mathbf S_u) rS_u^i\\
            &+ \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^k \sum_{j=1}^k \frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial S^i \partial S^j}(u, \mathbf S_u) \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij} S_u^i S_u^j
        \bigg) \,\mathrm du
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The steps from here are identical to the one-dimensional case, where we can take expectations of both sides and use the second order Taylor expansion of the exponential to get the variational inequality

\begin{equation}
    rv(t, \mathbf S) \ge \frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(t, \mathbf S) + \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{\partial v}{\partial S^i}(t, \mathbf S) rS^i + \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^k \sum_{j=1}^k \frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial S^i \partial S^j}(t, \mathbf S) \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij} S^i S^j
\end{equation}

where to simplify into the PINN framework as in (\ref{eq:pinn_pde}), we can define the operator $\mathcal L$ as

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L[v] = rv(t, \mathbf S) - \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{\partial v}{\partial S^i}(t, \mathbf S) rS^i - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^k \sum_{j=1}^k \frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial S^i \partial S^j}(t, \mathbf S) \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij} S^i S^j
\end{equation}

and the problem reduces to the same variational inequality as in the one-dimensional case (\ref{eq:variational_inequality_bs_1d}), with a different operator $\mathcal L$.

To finish the PINN formulation, we need to derive the boundary conditions. The terminal condition is straightforward, as it is given by the payoff function:
\begin{equation}
    v(T, \mathbf S) = g(\mathbf S) = \left(K - \prod_{i=1}^k S^i\right)^+
\end{equation}

For the spatial boundary conditions, we will again truncate the domain to
\begin{equation}
    \Omega = \left \{
        \mathbf S=(S^i, \dots, S^k) \in \mathbb R^k: S^i \in [S_\text{min}^i, S_\text{max}^i] \text{ for } i = 1, \dots, k
    \right \}
\end{equation}
for some sufficiently small and large respective minimum and maximum values for each stock. The lower boundary will be sampled from the set
\begin{equation}
    \partial_\text{lower} \Omega = \left\{
        \mathbf S \in \Omega: S^i = S_\text{min}^i \text{ for some } i
    \right\}
\end{equation}
where we have the boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v(t, \mathbf S) = g(\mathbf S), \qquad \mathbf S \in \partial_\text{lower} \Omega
\end{equation}
and the upper boundary will be sampled from the set
\begin{equation}
    \partial_\text{upper} \Omega = \left\{
    \mathbf S \in \Omega: S^i = S_\text{max}^i \text{ for some } i
    \right\}
\end{equation}
with the boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v(t, \mathbf S) = 0, \qquad \mathbf S \in \partial_\text{upper} \Omega
\end{equation}
analogous to the one-dimensional case. Of course, this framework could be easily adapted to other payoff functions, where the boundary conditions would change accordingly (but not the variational inequality). But for our benchmark, this is the setup we will use.

\chapter{Black-Scholes Model}

The Black-Scholes model \cite{black1973pricing} is the fundamental model for option pricing, which assumes that the underlying asset price follows a geometric Brownian motion. As with the market introduced in Section \ref{sec:risk-neutral-pricing}, the market consists of two underlying assets:

\begin{itemize}
    \item The risk-free asset, described by a deterministic function
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm d B_t = rB_t \mathrm dt
    \label{eq:bs_risk_free_asset}
    \end{equation}
    where for convenience, we set $B_0 = 1$ and $r$ is the constant risk-free interest rate with continuous compounding. This has the solution $B_t = e^{rt}$ for $t \ge 0$.
    \item The risky asset $S_t$, which has the price process of a geometric Brownian motion (GBM), which satisfies the stochastic differential equation
    \begin{equation}
        \mathrm dS_t = \mu S_t \mathrm dt + \sigma S_t \mathrm dW_t \qquad 0 \le t \le T
    \end{equation}
    for a given drift $\mu$ and volatility $\sigma$. We can solve this SDE to yield the unique solution
    \begin{equation}
        S_t = S_0 \exp \left(\left(\mu - \frac 12 \sigma^2\right) t + \sigma W_t\right)\qquad 0 \le t \le T
    \label{eq:gbm_closed_form}
    \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

For convenience, we will refer to the risk-free asset as the bond, and the risky asset as simply the asset for the remainder of this chapter.

Under risk-neutral pricing, we can find an equivalent probability measure $\mathbb Q$ under which the discounted asset price is a martingale, and hence all assets earn the risk-free rate in expectation after discounting. Under the risk-neutral measure, the SDE for the asset becomes
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm dS_t = r S_t \mathrm dt + \sigma S_t \mathrm dW_t^\mathbb Q
\end{equation}
where the drift has been replaced by the risk-free rate $r$. The derivation is done via Girsanov's theorem, and can be found in the appendix \ref{sec:change_of_measure}.

\section{Closed-form solution for European options}

Our first benchmark will be the closed-form solution for the European option, which would be used to evaluate the accuracy of the binomial tree later. The derivation of this formula is widely available and hence we will only include the main ideas. Using the risk-neutral pricing formula, the value of the European call option with strike $K$ and expiry $T$ is given by
\begin{equation}
    v(0, S_0) = e^{-rT}\mathbb E^\mathbb Q\big[(S_T - K)^+ \mid S_0\big]
\label{eq:rnpf_black_scholes}
\end{equation}

We use the solution of geometric brownian motion (\ref{eq:gbm_closed_form}) to note that
\begin{equation}
    S_T = S_0 \exp \left( (r-\tfrac 12 \sigma^2)T + \sigma W_T^{\mathbb Q}\right)
\end{equation}
under the risk-neutral measure $\mathbb Q$, having replaced the drift $\mu$ with the risk-free rate $r$. The Brownian motion $W_T^{\mathbb Q}$ has zero mean and variance $T$, so it can be written as $\sqrt T Z$, where we have the standard normal variable $Z \sim \mathcal N(0, 1)$. Therefore, $S_T$ becomes
\begin{equation}
    S_T = S_0 \exp \left( (r-\tfrac 12 \sigma^2)T + \sigma \sqrt{T} Z\right)
\end{equation}

This can be plugged into the risk-neutral pricing formula (\ref{eq:rnpf_black_scholes}): the exercise event $\{S_T > K\}$ is equivalent to $\{Z > -d_2\}$ where
\begin{equation}
    d_2 = \frac{\log(S_0/K) + (r - \tfrac 12 \sigma^2)T}{\sigma \sqrt T}, \qquad d_1 = d_2 + \sigma \sqrt T
\end{equation}

The expectation (\ref{eq:rnpf_black_scholes}) can be split, where we denote $\boldsymbol 1_{A}$ the indicator variable for the event $A$:
\begin{align}
    v(0, S_0) &= e^{-rT} \mathbb E^\mathbb Q[(S_T - K)^+] \\
    &= e^{-rT} \mathbb E^\mathbb Q[(S_T - K) \boldsymbol{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}] \\
    &= e^{-rT} \mathbb E^\mathbb Q[S_T \boldsymbol{1}_{\{S_T > K\}}] - Ke^{-rT} \mathbb Q(S_T > K)
\end{align}

The second probability is immediate as we see $\mathbb Q(S_T > K) = \mathbb Q(Z > d_2) = \Phi(d_2)$ by symmetry, where $\Phi$ is the CDF of the standard normal. We can complete the square in the Gaussian integrand for the first expectation to get the final formula \cite{black1973pricing}
\begin{equation}
    v(0, S_0) = S_0 \Phi(d_1) - Ke^{-rT}\Phi(d_2)
\end{equation}

\section{Binomial Tree Method}

We can use the binomial discrete-time model to price both European and American options with the tree method. This model uses the simplification that the asset price can only move to one of two possible new prices in a time step. To avoid explosion in the number of nodes, we will only consider recombinant trees so that the algorithm performs with quadratic time complexity (look back at Figure \ref{fig:recombinant_trees} for an illustration of recombinant trees). We will verify the approximated solution's validity against the closed-form formula for European options, and use its American variant as a benchmark for later methods.

To formalise the tree construction, let’s fix a time horizon $T > 0$. For some depth $n \ge 1$, divide the interval $[0, T]$ into $n$ even steps each of length $\Delta t = T/n$. Our asset $S_t$, where $t$ is now a discrete time index, will follow a multiplicative binomial evolution:
\begin{equation}
    S_{i+1} = \begin{cases}
    S_i u & \text{with probability }p \\S_i d & \text{with probability }1-p
    \end{cases}\qquad S_0 > 0
\end{equation}
where $u$ and $d$ are the model parameters determining the two factors of the asset price change per time step.

As with (\ref{eq:bs_risk_free_asset}), the financial market will also consist of a risk-free asset $B_t$, discretised so that it satisfies
\begin{equation}
    B_{i+1} = B_i e^{r\Delta t}, \qquad B_0 = 1
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the continuously-compounded risk-free rate, typically considered interest. For an arbitrage free market, we should additionally ensure that
\begin{equation}
d < e^{r\Delta t} < u
\end{equation}
for sufficiently small $\Delta t$, else we can make a riskless profit by shorting the asset and buying the bond, or vice versa.

To employ risk-neutral pricing, we additionally require the discounted asset price to have zero drift (admitting a martingale measure). More precisely, let the discounted price be, with the bond as numeraire:
\begin{equation}
\tilde S_i = \frac{S_i}{B_i} = e^{-ri\Delta t} S_i
\end{equation}
We require some probability measure $\mathbb Q$, equivalent to the physical measure $\mathbb P$ (have the same non-zero support) such that
\begin{equation}
\mathbb E^\mathbb Q [\tilde S_{i+1} \mid \mathcal F_i] = \tilde S_i
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal F_i$ is the filtration at time step $i$.

To calculate the risk-neutral probability, we can apply this to our model:
\begin{equation}
e^{-r(i+1)\Delta t}\big[pS_i u + (1-p)S_i d \big] = e^{-ri\Delta t}S_i
\end{equation}

Collect the $p$ terms and cancel out the discount factor to get
\begin{equation}
p(S_i u - S_i d) + S_i d = e^{r\Delta t} S_i
\end{equation}

Divide both sides by $S_i$ and rearrange to make $p$ the subject to obtain
\begin{equation}
p = \frac{e^{r \Delta t} -d}{u-d}
\end{equation}

This is what we will use in our binomial model.

To price the option at time $0$, we will perform backward induction from expiry, since we know that the price is just the payoff $g: \mathbb R^+ \to \mathbb R$ as the option expires. As the discounted price process is a martingale under the risk-free measure $\mathbb Q$, the arbitrage-free value of the derivative at time $i \Delta t$ is necessarily
\begin{equation}
v(t = i\Delta t) = \underbrace{e^{-r(T-i\Delta t)}}_\text{discount} \,\mathbb E^\mathbb Q[g(S_T) \mid \mathcal F_i]
\end{equation}

We will utilise the Cox-Ross-Rubinstein (CRR) Model \cite{cox1979option} for our implementation, which chooses the parameters
\begin{equation}
u = e^{\sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}}\qquad d = e^{-\sigma \sqrt{\Delta t}}
\end{equation}
satisfying the arbitrage free condition, and normalising the local variance of returns to the volatility parameter $\sigma^2$. They also conveniently multiply to $1$.

To implement this, we need to first compute the price tree. This would be, for the price $S_{i, j}$ at time step $i$ after $j$ up-moves (so clearly $i \ge j$)
\begin{equation}
S_{i, j} = S_0 u^j d^{i-j}
\end{equation}

As the payoff is known at maturity, we can simply set, with $v_{i, j}$ the value of the option at time step $i$ with $j$ up-moves:
\begin{equation}
v_{n, j} = g(S_{n, j}) \qquad 0 \le j \le n
\end{equation}
and recursively set
\begin{equation}
v_{i, j} = e^{-r\Delta t} \big(p v_{i+1, j+1} + (1-p)v_{i+1, j}\big)
\end{equation}
for $0 \le j \le i$. This would be it for European options, but since early exercise is allowed, we will compare this value with the payoff of the price at step $i$ with $j$ up-moves, which is just $f(S_{i, j})$. Thus the value is given by
\begin{equation}
v_{i, j} = \max\bigg(e^{-r\Delta t} \big(p v_{i+1, j+1} + (1-p)v_{i+1, j}\big), \; g(S_{i, j})\bigg)
\end{equation}
The root value $v_{0, 0}$ would be the arbitrage-free option price.

\section{Physics-Informed Neural Networks}

In order to use the framework of PINNs as detailed in Section \ref{sec:pinns}, we need to first derive the PDE satisfied by the option price, as well as the boundary conditions. We will first work with the one-dimensional case, then discuss the extension to the multi-dimensional case and how we could build a benchmark to against which we evaluate our results.

\subsection{PINN formulation for one-dimensional case}

In order to set up our PINN, we need to first derive the PDE satisfied by the option price. Recall the value function $v: [0, T] \times \mathbb R^+ \to \mathbb R$ of an American put with payoff $g: \mathbb R^+ \to \mathbb R$ at time $t$ with asset price $S_t = S$ is given by

\begin{equation}
    v(t, S) = \sup_{\tau \in [t, T]} \mathbb E\big[e^{-r(\tau - t)}g(S_\tau) \mid S_t = S\big]
\label{eq:american_option_value_function}
\end{equation}

where $\tau$ is any stopping time in $[t, T]$ and for convenience we will simply denote $\mathbb E$ is conditional expectation at time $t$, given $S_t = S$, in this section. For an American option, we always have two options:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Immediately exercise the option and get payoff $g(S)$. Since this is not necessarily optimal, the payoff must bound the value option from below:
    \begin{equation}
        v(t, S) \ge g(S)
    \end{equation}
    \item Do nothing and let the option continue to time $t + h$ with $h>0$ a very small number. By using the supremum definition of $v(t, S)$ (\ref{eq:american_option_value_function}) and swapping the expectation and supremum \cite{zheng2025fundamentals} we arrive at
    \begin{equation}
        e^{rh}v(t, S) \ge \mathbb E \big[v(t+h, S_{t + h})\big]
    \label{eq:continuation_value_inequality}
    \end{equation}
    and the inequality turns into an equality at the limit $\delta \to 0$.
    
\end{itemize}

We will additionally assume that the value function $v \in \mathcal C^{1,2}$ (continuously differentiable with respect to $t$ and twice continuously differentiable with respect to $S$) so that we can apply Itô's formula. Let's model the asset price $S$ as a geometric Brownian motion satisfying under the risk-neutral measure
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm dS_t = rS_t \mathrm dt + \sigma S_t \mathrm dW_t
\end{equation}

Then by Ito’s lemma and taking expectations of both sides we have
\begin{equation}
    \mathbb E\big[v(t +\delta, S_{t+h})\big] = v(t, S) + \mathbb E\bigg[\int_t^{t+h} \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(u, S_u) + \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(u, S_u) r S_u + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(u, S_u) \sigma^2 S_u^2\right)\mathrm du\bigg]
\end{equation}

Now let’s substitute (\ref{eq:continuation_value_inequality}) into the LHS, which gives us
\begin{equation}
    e^{rh}v(t, S) \ge v(t, S) + \mathbb E\bigg[\int_t^{t+h} \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(u, S_u) + \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(u, S_u) r S_u + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(u, S_u) \sigma^2 S_u^2\right)\mathrm du\bigg]
\end{equation}

We can use the Taylor approximation $e^{rh} = 1 + rh + o(h)$, which permits us to cancel the $v(t, S)$ on both sides. Dividing both sides by $h$ in the same step, we get
\begin{equation}
    rv(t, S) + \frac{o(h)}{h} \ge \mathbb E\bigg[\frac 1h\int_t^{t+h} \left(\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(u, S_u) + \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(u, S_u) r S_u + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(u, S_u) \sigma^2 S_u^2\right)\mathrm du\bigg]
\end{equation}

Sending $h \to 0$ makes the higher order term $o(h)/h$ vanish, and the expectation of the integral to collapse to the integrand. Thus we obtain
\begin{equation}
    rv(t, S) \ge \frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(t, S) + \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(t, S) r S + \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(t, S) \sigma^2 S^2
\end{equation}

or for simplicity, we define the linear parabolic operator $\mathcal L$ \cite{zhao2025fractional}:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L[v] = rv(t, S) - \frac{\partial v}{\partial S}(t, S) r S - \frac{1}{2}\frac{\partial ^2v}{\partial S^2}(t, S) \sigma^2 S^2
\label{eq:bs_operator}
\end{equation}

Thus we can concisely express this inequality as
\begin{equation}
    \min\left(-\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(t, S) + \mathcal L[v], v(t, S) - g(S)\right) \ge 0
\end{equation}

However, note that at time $t$, we must either exercise the option or do nothing and continue. One of these two options must be optimal, implying that one of the arguments must be $0$. Thus we actually have an equality:
\begin{equation}
    \min\left(-\frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(t, S) + \mathcal L[v], v(t, S) - g(S)\right) = 0
\label{eq:variational_inequality_bs_1d}
\end{equation}
which is the variational equation \cite{jo2019variational} for the American option. Note this is exactly the formulation of the PINN problem (\ref{eq:pinn_pde}).

We are now ready to derive the boundary conditions. Our option has a terminal payoff at time $T$ given by $g(S)$, so we have the terminal condition
\begin{equation}
    v(T, S) = g(S)
\end{equation}
which satisfies the form of the terminal condition for PINNs (\ref{eq:pinn_terminal}). For the spatial boundary conditions, we have two boundaries to consider: $S \to 0$ and $S \to \infty$. In practice, we will have to truncate the domain to $\Omega = (S_\text{min}, S_\text{max})$ for some sufficiently small $S_\text{min}$ and sufficiently large $S_\text{max}$. At the lower boundary, we have the boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v(t, S_\text{min}) = g(S_\text{min})
\end{equation}
because it is always optimal to exercise the option when the asset is at its lowest price. At the upper boundary, we assume the option is too far out of the money to ever finish in the money, and hence the option price is approximately $0$. Thus we have the boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v(t, S_\text{max}) = 0
\end{equation}
These three conditions together would form the boundary setup (\ref{eq:pinn_boundary}) for our PINN.

\subsection{Multi-asset case}

We will now extend our PINN framework to the multi-dimensional case, where we have $n$ underlying assets, and hence the neural network $f$ will take $n+1$ inputs (time and the price of each asset). The variational inequality can be derived with a similar argument as in the one-dimensional case, but we will use the multi-dimensional case of Itô's lemma (Theorem \ref{thm:ito_lemma}). In order to be able to evaluate the accuracy of our results, we will consider a specific multi-asset payoff such that there exists an equivalent one-dimensional reduction. This allows us to use the binomial tree method (or indeed the one-dimensional PINN solution) as a benchmark, giving us confidence for the PINN solution on more complex payoffs.

In the multi-dimensional case, we have $n$ underlying assets with price processes following a correlated geometric Brownian motion. Mathematically, let each asset $S^i$ satisfy under the risk-free measure $\mathbb Q$, for $i = 1, \dots, n$,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm dS_t^i = rS_t^i\mathrm dt + \sigma_i S^i_t \mathrm dW_t^i
\end{equation}
with quadratic covariation $\mathrm d \langle W^i, W^j\rangle_t = \rho_{ij}\mathrm dt$ for all $i, j$ pairs.

\subsection{Reduction of multi-asset to one-asset case}

We will work with a specific payoff function that allows us to reduce the multi-dimensional case to a one-dimensional case, which would, in turn, give us confidence that our PINN implementation is correct, and we can change the payoff function to more complex ones and be reasonably assured that the results are still correct.

As the geometric Brownian motion is multiplicative, for our benchmark, we will consider the put on the product of the assets. More precisely, we consider the put option with payoff
\begin{equation}
    g(S^1, \dots, S^n) = \left(K - \prod_{i=1}^n S^i\right)^+
\end{equation}

To represent the dynamics of the product of the assets, we can apply Ito’s formula to the log of the price process. The log representation of the Itô process (refer to Definition \ref{def:ito_process}) is
\begin{equation}
\ln S_t^i = \ln S_0^t + \int_0^t \left (r - \frac 12 \sigma^2_i\right) \,\mathrm ds + \int_0^t \sigma _i \,\mathrm dW_s^i
\end{equation}

Define the new asset $X$ to be
\begin{equation}X_t := \prod_{i=1}^n S^i_t\end{equation}
and hence we have
\begin{equation}
\ln X_t = \sum_{i=1}^n \ln S_t^i = \ln X_0 + \int_0^t \left(nr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2 \right)\,\mathrm ds + \sum_{i=1}^n \int_0^t \sigma_i \,\mathrm dW_s^i
\end{equation}

By taking the differential we have
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d\ln X_t = \left(nr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2 \right)\,\mathrm dt + \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i \,\mathrm dW_s^i
\label{eq:d_ln_X_t}
\end{equation}

The quadratic variation of the martingale term is
\begin{equation}
\left\langle\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i W^i, \sum_{j=1}^n \sigma_j W^j\right\rangle_t = \sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n \sigma_i\sigma_j \rho_{ij} t =: \sigma^2_X t
\end{equation}
i.e. $\sigma_X^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^n \sigma_i\sigma_j\rho_{ij}$. So we can express the second term of (\ref{eq:d_ln_X_t}) in terms of a new Brownian motion $\widetilde W$:
\begin{equation}
\sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i \,\mathrm dW_s^i =  \sigma_X \mathrm d\widetilde W_t
\end{equation}

Going back, we have
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d \ln X_t = \left( nr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2\right)\,\mathrm dt + \sigma_X \mathrm d \widetilde W_t
\end{equation}

Exponentiating using Ito’s formula gets us
\begin{equation}
\mathrm dX_t = X_t\left[\left(nr -\frac12\sum_{i=1}^n\sigma_i^2\right)\mathrm dt + \sigma_X\mathrm d\widetilde W_t\right]
+ \frac12 X_t\sigma_X^2 \mathrm dt
\end{equation}
which we can rearrange to get
\begin{equation}
\mathrm d X_t = \left(nr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2 + \frac 12 \sigma_X^2\right) X_t \mathrm dt + \sigma _X X_t \,\mathrm d\widetilde W_t
\end{equation}
which is a geometric Brownian motion with volatility $\sigma_X$ and drift $r_X$ where
\begin{align}
    r_X &= nr - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2 + \frac 12 \sigma_X^2 \\
    &= nr + \sum_{i < j} \sigma_i\sigma_j\rho_{ij}
\end{align}
by matching coefficients. We can verify the equivalence by simulating many price paths for the assets and comparing their product against the simulations of price paths for the equivalent asset. Using the initial values (chosen arbitrariliy)
\begin{equation}
r = 0.05 \qquad S_0 = (10, 5, 3)^\top \qquad \sigma = (0.2, 0.3, 0.1)^\top \qquad \Sigma = \begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 0.6 & -0.2 \\ 0.6 & 1 & 0.4 \\-0.2 & 0.4 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\end{equation}
where $\Sigma$ is the correlation matrix, we can plot the histogram of final prices in Figure \ref{fig:gbm_histogram}.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../plots/black_scholes/gbm_histogram.png}
    \caption[Asset product price histogram]{Histogram of final prices for product of assets and equivalent asset}
    \label{fig:gbm_histogram}
\end{figure}

They look very similar, and we can further statistically test if they come from the same distribution. Using Kolmogorov-Smirnov's 2-sample test on the null hypothesis that they come from the same distribution, we obtain a p-value of $0.8491$, which does not provide sufficient evidence to reject the null hypothesis, which gives us stronger evidence that our derivation is correct.

Therefore, we will be able to price the put option on the product of multiple assets and compare that to the price of the put option on this single constructed asset as a benchmark.

\subsection{PINN formulation for multi-dimensional case}

With the benchmark in place, we now need to derive the variational inequality for the multi-dimensional case where we have $n$ assets. This is a straightforward extension of the one-dimensional case, where the value function $v(t, S^1, \dots, S^n)$ now takes $n+1$ inputs. For notation, we will express the multiple assets as a vector $S = (S^1, \dots, S^n)^\top$, so the value function can be written more concisely as $v(t, S)$. Similarly, the payoff function will be written as $g(S^1, \dots, S^n) = g(S)$.

The value function stays
\begin{equation}
    v(t, S) = \sup_{\tau \in [t, T]} \mathbb E\big[e^{-r(\tau - t)}g(S_\tau) \mid S_t = S\big]
\end{equation}

Should we choose not to exercise the option at time $t$, we can apply the same argument as in the one-dimensional case: considering a small $h > 0$, we can apply Itô's lemma on multiple dimensions to yield
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        v(t + h, S_{t + h}) = v(t, S) + \int_t^{t+h} &\bigg(
            \frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(u, S_u) +
            \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial v}{\partial S^i}(u, S_u) rS_u^i\\
            &+ \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial S^i \partial S^j}(u, S_u) \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij} S_u^i S_u^j
        \bigg) \,\mathrm du
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}

The steps from here are identical to the one-dimensional case, where we can take expectations of both sides and use the second order Taylor expansion of the exponential to get the variational inequality
\begin{equation}
    rv(t, S) \ge \frac{\partial v}{\partial t}(t, S) + \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial v}{\partial S^i}(t, S) rS^i + \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial S^i \partial S^j}(t, S) \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij} S^i S^j
\end{equation}
where to simplify into the PINN framework as in (\ref{eq:pinn_pde}), we can define the operator $\mathcal L$ as
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal L[v] = rv(t, S) - \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial v}{\partial S^i}(t, S) rS^i - \frac 12 \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial^2 v}{\partial S^i \partial S^j}(t, S) \sigma_i \sigma_j \rho_{ij} S^i S^j
\label{eq:bs_operator_multi}
\end{equation}
and the problem reduces to the same variational inequality as in the one-dimensional case (\ref{eq:variational_inequality_bs_1d}), with a different operator $\mathcal L$.

To finish the PINN formulation, we need to derive the boundary conditions. The terminal condition is straightforward, as it is given by the payoff function:
\begin{equation}
    v(T, S) = g(S) = \left(K - \prod_{i=1}^n S^i\right)^+
\end{equation}

For the spatial boundary conditions, we will again truncate the domain to
\begin{equation}
    \Omega = \left \{
        S=(S^i, \dots, S^n) \in \mathbb R^n: S^i \in (S_\text{min}^i, S_\text{max}^i) \text{ for } i = 1, \dots, n
    \right \}
\end{equation}
for some sufficiently small and large respective minimum and maximum values for each asset. The lower boundary will be sampled from the set
\begin{equation}
    \partial_\text{lower} \Omega = \left\{
        S \in \Omega: S^i = S_\text{min}^i \text{ for some } i
    \right\}
\end{equation}
where we have the boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v(t, S) = g(S), \qquad S \in \partial_\text{lower} \Omega
\end{equation}
and the upper boundary will be sampled from the set
\begin{equation}
    \partial_\text{upper} \Omega = \left\{
    S \in \Omega: S^i = S_\text{max}^i \text{ for some } i
    \right\}
\end{equation}
with the boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v(t, S) = 0, \qquad S \in \partial_\text{upper} \Omega
\end{equation}
analogous to the one-dimensional case. Of course, this framework could be easily adapted to other payoff functions, where the boundary conditions would change accordingly (but not the variational inequality). This will be explored later in the numerical results when we consider a different payoff. However, for our benchmark, the put on the product of assets is the setup we will use.

\section{Sobolev Deep Neural Networks}

For the Black-Scholes model, we will additionally explore a different method to price American puts with neural networks, but this time instead of the traditional boundary conditions, we will work with fractional Sobolev norms to construct our loss function. This is because it has been shown that conditional on the loss converging to $0$, the neural network will converge to the true solution \cite{zhao2025fractional}, which is a stronger guarantee than just pointwise convergence. Additionally, the loss function components no longer depend on the payoff and is applicable for all situations, a desirable property for a general method.

The theory of Sobolev spaces is rich and we will not attempt to cover all the details, to avoid diverting attention from the main focus of the project. However, we make particular use of fractional Sobolev spaces, which are a generalisation of the integer order Sobolev spaces. These definitions are taken from \cite{adams2003sobolev, di2012hitchhikers} which are very good resources for the underlying theory.

\subsection{Sobolev spaces}

Throughout this section, let $\Omega \subset \mathbb R^n$ be an open bounded set with Lipschitz boundary, on which our target function is defined, and let $p \in [1, +\infty)$ be the integrability parameter. Recall the Lebesgue space
\begin{equation}
    L^p(\Omega) := \left \{u: \Omega \to \mathbb R : u \text{ is measurable and } \int_\Omega |u(x)|^p \,\mathrm dx < \infty\right\}
\end{equation}
and the integer order Sobolev space $W^{m, p}(\Omega)$ for $m \in \mathbb N$ \cite{adams2003sobolev},
\begin{equation}
    W^{m, p}(\Omega) := \left\{u \in L^p(\Omega) : D^\alpha u \in L^p(\Omega), \ |\alpha| \le m\right\},
\end{equation}
where $D^\alpha u$ denotes the weak derivative of $u$ with respect to the multi-index $\alpha$.

The fractional Sobolev space $W^{s, p}(\Omega)$ is defined with the fractional exponent $s \in (0, 1)$ as \cite{di2012hitchhikers}:
\begin{equation}
    W^{s, p}(\Omega) := \left\{u \in L^p(\Omega) : \frac{|u(x) - u(y)|}{|x-y|^{n/p + s}} \in L^p(\Omega \times \Omega)\right\},
\end{equation}
which can be interpreted as an intermediate Banach space between $L^p(\Omega)$ and $W^{1, p}(\Omega)$, endowed with the norm
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        \|u\|^p_{W^{s, p}(\Omega)} &= \|u\|^p_{L^p(\Omega)} + [u]^p_{W^{s, p}(\Omega)} \\
        &= \int_\Omega |u|^p \,\mathrm dx + \int_\Omega \int_\Omega \frac{|u(x)-u(y)|^p}{|x-y|^{n+sp}}\,\mathrm dx \, \mathrm dy,
    \end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where we have used the \textit{Gagliardo seminorm} of $u$:
\begin{equation}
    [u]_{W^{s, p}(\Omega)} := \left( \int_\Omega \int_\Omega \frac{|u(x)-u(y)|^p}{|x-y|^{n+sp}}\,\mathrm dx \, \mathrm dy\right)^{1/p}.
\end{equation}

We can extend this definition to non-integer $s > 1$. In the decomposition $s = m + \sigma$ with $m \in \mathbb N$ and $\sigma \in (0, 1)$, we define
\begin{equation}
    W^{s, p}(\Omega) := \left \{ u \in W^{m, p}(\Omega) : D^\alpha u \in W^{\sigma, p}(\Omega), \ |\alpha| = m \right\}
\end{equation}
endowed with the natural norm
\begin{equation}
    \|u\|^p_{W^{s,p}(\Omega)} := \|u\|^p_{W^{m,p}(\Omega)} + \sum_{|\alpha| = m} [D^\alpha u]^p_{W^{\sigma, p}(\Omega)}.
\end{equation}

In the special case $p=2$, the fractional Sobolev space becomes a Hilbert space, which we denote $H^s(\Omega) := W^{s, 2}(\Omega)$. This is the specific case we will be working with in our loss construction.

\subsection{Model}

Suppose we would like to price an option on $n$ assets $S = (S^1, \dots, S^n)$. As asset prices are theoretically unbounded, we truncate it in our model much like the vanilla PINN. Thus the domain of the target function can be chosen to be the open set
\begin{equation}
    \Omega = (S_\text{min}^1, S_\text{max}^1) \times \cdots \times (S_\text{min}^n, S_\text{max}^n)
\end{equation}

In our study we will make the simplification that $S_\text{min}^i = S_\text{min}^j$ and $S_\text{max}^i = S_\text{max}^j$ for all pairs $i, j$, which we will denote with $a$ and $b$ respectively. Our simplified spaces are therefore
\begin{equation}
    \Omega = (a, b)^n \qquad \Omega_T = (0, T) \times \Omega \qquad \Sigma = (0, T) \times \Gamma
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma := \partial \Omega$ is the boundary of $\Omega$, a hyper-rectangle. We will use the representation
\begin{equation}
    \Gamma = \bigcup_{i=1}^n \big(\{x \in [a, b]^n : x_i = a\} \cup \{x \in [a, b]^n : x_i = b\} \big)
\end{equation}
which is not the simplest representation, but it is the most useful for our purposes as it is decomposed into faces, needed for the normal derivative. Note that in the one-dimensional case, the boundary $\Gamma$ reduces simply to the endpoints $\Gamma = \{a, b\}$.

\subsection{Loss function}
Our loss function would be a weighted sum of four loss components $\sum_{i=1}^4 \lambda_iJ_i$, each of which ensures regulation of a specific aspect of our function, with the weights either kept constant or chosen through adaptive loss weighting as presented in Section \ref{sec:adaptive_loss_weighting}. We present the generalised loss construction, applicable for any dimension.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The variational loss: this is as before, controlling the accuracy of our function in the interior of our domain:
    \begin{equation}
        J_1 = \|\min \{\mathcal G[f], f-g\}\|^2_{L^2(\Omega_T)}
    \end{equation}

    \item The energy loss: before we were only controlling the L2 norm of the payoff, but we require the option value to equal the payoff as a function and not just pointwise. Thus we need to incorporate the gradient into the loss so that the spatial slope of the payoff is respected.
    \begin{equation}
        J_2 = \|f(0, \cdot) - g(\cdot) \|^2_{H^1(\Omega)}
    \end{equation}
    where we have the norm defined as
    \begin{equation}
        \|d\|^2_{H^1(\Omega), \mu_2} = \|d\|^2_{L^2(\Omega)} + \|\nabla d\|^2_{L^2(\Omega)}
    \end{equation}
    and the derivative
    \begin{equation}\nabla d = \big(\partial_{x_1}d, \dots, \partial_{x_n}d\big)\end{equation}

    \item The Dirichlet trace loss: this enforces $f = g$ on the spatial boundary $\Sigma$.
    \begin{equation}
        J_3 =\|f(t, x) - g( x)\|^2_{H^{\frac 34, \frac 32}(\Sigma)}
    \end{equation}
    where
    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}
            \|d\|^2_{H^{\frac 34, \frac 32}(\Sigma)} = \|d\|^2_{H^{0,1}(\Sigma)} &+ \int_0^T\int_0^T\frac{\|d(t_1, \cdot) - d(t_2, \cdot)\|^2_{L^2(\Gamma)}}{|t_1-t_2|^{2.5}}\,\mathrm dt_1\,\mathrm dt_2 \\ &+ \int_0^T\int_\Gamma\int_\Gamma \frac{|d(t, x) - d(t, y)|^2}{\|x-y\|^{n}}\,\mathrm d\sigma(x)\mathrm d\sigma(y) \mathrm dt
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}
    The double time integral penalises the variation of $f$ between two time points, and the double space integral penalises the variation of $f$ between two spatial points.

    The time exponent $2.5$ is equal to $2p+1$ where $p$ is the decimal part of the time fractional exponent, and the space exponent is equal to $2\theta + n - 1$ where $\theta$ is the decimal part of the space fractional exponent, and $n$ the dimension. Note that here $\mathrm d \sigma(x)$ and $\mathrm d \sigma(y)$ indicate the surface measures on $\Gamma$, but in practice we can treat these as $\mathrm dx$ and $\mathrm dy$.

    \item The Neumann trace loss: this ensures the normal derivative of $f-g$ vanishes on the boundary $\Sigma$.
    \begin{equation}
        J_4 =\left\|\nabla (f-g) \cdot \nu \right\|^2_{H^{\frac 14, \frac 12}(\Sigma)}
    \end{equation}
    where the normal derivative is $\nabla(f-g)\cdot \nu = \partial_{x_{i^\star}}(f-g)$ with $x_{i^\star}$ the coordinate normal to the face of the boundary we are on, justifying the choice of the representation of $\Gamma$ as a union of faces. Rigorously, we require the sign, but in practice this is not necessary, as we will take the square in the end. The fractional norm is thus written
    \begin{equation}
        \begin{aligned}
            \|d\|^2_{H^{\frac 14, \frac 12}(\Sigma)} = \|d\|^2_{L^2(\Sigma)} &+ \int_0^T\int_0^T \frac{\|d(t_1, \cdot) - d(t_2, \cdot)\|^2_{L^2(\Gamma)}}{|t_1-t_2|^{1.5}}\,\mathrm dt_1\mathrm dt_2 \\ &+ \int_{0}^T\int_\Gamma\int_\Gamma \frac{|d(t, x) - d(t, y)|^2}{\|x-y\|^n}\,\mathrm d\sigma(x)\mathrm d\sigma (y)\mathrm dt
        \end{aligned}
    \end{equation}
    because $H^{0, 0} = L^2$.
\end{enumerate}

The third and fourth losses are those that enforce $f$ to solve the option pricing variational inequality with regularity in the time and space dimensions beyond pointwise \cite{lions2012non}. In practice, we will compute a Monte Carlo estimate of these integrals by sampling pairs $(t_1, t_2)$ from the domain, taking care that they are not too close to each other by rejecting pairs that have a distance less than a threshold. We then evaluate the integrand and take the mean across all pairs.

\section{Minimum of two assets}
To demonstrate the flexibility of our PINN framework, we will now shift our focus onto different payoffs on multiple assets as an extension. \cite{detemple2005american} explores a variety of different payoffs for American options on multiple assets, but that with a particularly interesting free boundary is the one of the call on the maximum of two assets. We will deal with the analogous case for the put on the minimum of two assets.

To price this, we can simply change the payoff function in our loss function, and update the boundary conditions accordingly to the new payoff function, but the variational inequality and the rest of the framework remain unchanged. For the American put on the minimum of two assets, the payoff function is given by $g\left(S^1, S^2\right) = \left(K - \min(S^1, S^2)\right)^+$, and thus we must have the terminal boundary condition
\begin{equation}
    v\left(T, S^1, S^2\right) = \left(K - \min(S^1, S^2)\right)^+
\end{equation}
When one of the assets is very high (i.e., $S^1 \to \infty$ or $S^2 \to \infty$), the minimum of the two assets is just the other asset, and thus we have
\begin{equation}
    v\left(t, \infty, S^2\right) = v_\text{1D}\left(t, S^2\right) \qquad v\left(t, S^1, \infty\right) = v_\text{1D}\left(t, S^1\right)
\end{equation}
where $v_\text{1D}$ is the price of the one-dimensional American put option on a single asset, which we can compute with the different methods we have already explored. Finally, when one of the assets is worthless, the minimum of the two assets is just the worthless asset, and hence the value will be $K$ as we should exercise immediately. Thus, the boundary condition would be
\begin{equation}
    v\left(t, S^1, S^2\right) = K \qquad \text{ for } S^1 = 0 \text{ or } S^2 = 0
\end{equation}
Together, these boundary conditions (alongside the variational inequality which remains the same with the operator $\mathcal L$ as in \ref{eq:bs_operator_multi}) form the PINN framework which we can train.

In Figure \ref{fig:bs_min_heatmap}, we can see that our PINN yields results that are in line with our expectations. We expect to see some symmetry along the $S^1 = S^2$ line as the payoff is symmetric in the two assets. We introduce some asymmetry by training the network where $S^2$ has a higher constant volatility than $S^1$.

We train the model with a high volatility of $(\sigma_1, \sigma_2) = (0.4, 0.5)$ to make the theta decay more significant. This also gives us a clearer evolution of the free boundary, which we see in Figure \ref{fig:bs_min_continuation_probs}, where we can see that not only the continuation region is a mirror image of the theoretical result described in \cite{detemple2005american} as we would expect, but the PINN also captures the shrinking nature of the continuation region as we approach maturity, consistent with the intuition that the option loses time value and the region would close entirely, leaving only the box $\{(S^1, S^2) : S^1 \ge K, S^2 \ge K\}$ as the continuation region at maturity.

Overall, this demonstrates the capacity of our PINN approach to pricing options and we can see that the framework is flexible enough to be applied to different payoffs with minimal adjustments, yielding convincing results that are consistent with theoretical results.
